## 1. Purpose of Drawing Preprocess

This drawing preprocess translates architectural drawings into textual information for Precedent DNA analysis.

The purpose is to extract:

1. **Architectural vocabulary**  
   Important spatial elements, housing blocks, sky plaza, circulation cores, unit types, facade groups, and collective spaces.

2. **Spatial relationship descriptions**  
   How apartments, corridors, stairs, cores, sky plaza, facade groups, voids, and neighbourhood blocks relate to each other.

3. **Overall spatial logic**  
   The design logic behind Mirador as a vertical social housing block made of stacked neighbourhoods and a lifted courtyard.

---

## 2. Drawing Inventory

### 2.1 Site Plan

The site plan shows the building placed on an irregular urban plot surrounded by roads and open urban space. The tower stands as a compact block object in a district of large-scale residential planning.

The site plan reveals that Mirador does not complete a traditional perimeter block at ground level. Instead, it acts as a vertical object that concentrates housing density upward and preserves open ground around it.

### 2.2 Multi-Level Floor Plan Drawing

The floor plan drawing shows several levels of the building, including ground level and upper residential floors.

The plans reveal:

- Long horizontal residential bars
- Multiple apartment types
- Repeated but varied residential units
- Vertical circulation cores
- Stairs and elevator zones
- Shared corridors
- Voids and breaks in the block
- Changing layouts at different levels

The building is not organized by one identical typical floor. Instead, different levels contain different housing groups and circulation arrangements.

### 2.3 Exterior Photo

The exterior photo shows the building as a large vertical frame with a central void. The facade is divided into different material and color fields. The red circulation zones stand out as vertical cuts and public routes.

The exterior confirms the main spatial logic:

**Stacked Housing Blocks → Central Sky Plaza → Vertical Neighbourhood Landmark**

## 5. Spatial Relationship Descriptions

### 5.1 Housing Blocks → stack into → Vertical Neighbourhood

Different housing groups are stacked together to create a vertical community.

### 5.2 Sky Plaza → replaces → Ground Courtyard

The traditional courtyard is lifted into the air and transformed into an elevated communal lookout.

### 5.3 Central Void → frames → Guadarrama View

The large opening in the building frames distant views toward the mountains.

### 5.4 Mini Neighbourhoods → create → Housing Diversity

The building is divided into smaller residential groups with varied unit types and identities.

### 5.5 Circulation Cores → connect → Housing Groups

Elevators, stairs, corridors, and red circulation paths connect the stacked housing groups.

### 5.6 Red Circulation → marks → Vertical Itinerary

The red internal routes make movement through the building visible and memorable.

### 5.7 Facade Materials → differentiate → Neighbourhood Groups

Different colors, materials, and window patterns identify different parts of the housing block.

### 5.8 Apartments → occupy → Perimeter Blocks

Housing units occupy the perimeter of each block, gaining light, air, and views.

### 5.9 Sky Plaza → provides → Community Garden

The elevated void provides residents with an outdoor social and garden space.

### 5.10 Lift Connection → links → Ground Plaza and Sky Plaza

A direct lift connection allows residents and visitors to access the elevated communal space.

### 5.11 Vertical Alleys → perforate → Tower Volume

Circulation and voids cut through the mass, preventing it from becoming a solid block.

### 5.12 Stacked Housing Types → challenge → Standard Unit Repetition

The project rejects the idea that social housing must use one repeated standard unit.

## 6. Overall Spatial Logic

Mirador’s spatial logic is based on turning the city block into a vertical neighbourhood.

The typological sequence can be summarized as:

**Closed Urban Block → Lifted Courtyard → Vertical Superblock → Sky Plaza → Social Housing Landmark**

The residential sequence can be summarized as:

**Apartment Unit → Housing Group → Mini Neighbourhood → Stacked Tower → Collective Sky Plaza**

The circulation sequence can be summarized as:

**Ground Plaza → Lift / Red Circulation → Vertical Alleys → Housing Groups → Sky Plaza**

Through this strategy, Mirador combines:

1. **Social housing density**
2. **Multiple housing types**
3. **Elevated communal space**
4. **View-oriented sky plaza**
5. **Facade differentiation**
6. **Vertical circulation as urban experience**
7. **Urban landmark identity**

The project demonstrates how a social housing tower can act as a vertical urban block rather than a repetitive slab.

---

## 7. Design Interpretation

The drawing analysis suggests that Mirador is a project about rethinking collective housing as a vertical city fragment.

The most important design interpretation is:

**The courtyard becomes a sky plaza.**

The project does not simply stack apartments. It transforms the traditional courtyard block by lifting the communal void into the air. This elevated void becomes a collective garden, lookout, and identity device.

The second important interpretation is:

**The tower is made of neighbourhoods, not identical units.**

The plans, facade materials, and circulation paths show that the building is divided into housing groups with different identities. This creates a social and visual alternative to standardized housing repetition.
