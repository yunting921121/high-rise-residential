## 1. Purpose of Photos and Renders Preprocess

This preprocess translates photos and visual materials into textual descriptions for architectural precedent analysis.

The goal is to extract:

1. **Design vocabulary**  
   Visible architectural elements, facade groups, central void, sky plaza, circulation elements, and social housing identity.

2. **Atmospheric description**  
   The visual and spatial atmosphere created by the large opening, patchwork facade, stacked blocks, and red circulation cores.

3. **Design intent reading**  
   The possible design intention behind creating a vertical neighbourhood in a repetitive residential district.

4. **Contextual relationship**  
   How the building relates to Sanchinarro, peripheral Madrid, social housing policy, and the need for collective space.

## 3. Atmospheric Description

Mirador Building creates the atmosphere of a vertical neighbourhood inserted into a repetitive housing district.

From the exterior photo, the building appears as a large block lifted and carved open. The central void is the most powerful visual element. It turns the building from a solid tower into a frame for sky, city, and landscape.

The atmosphere can be described through five qualities:

### 3.1 Monumental Social Housing

The project gives social housing a strong public image. It is not hidden or anonymous; it becomes a landmark.

### 3.2 Lifted Public Life

The sky plaza suggests that collective space can exist high above the ground, not only at street level.

### 3.3 Patchwork Community

The facade appears as a patchwork of different materials and window patterns, expressing the idea of multiple neighbourhoods inside one tower.

### 3.4 Urban Critique

The building visually contrasts with surrounding repetitive housing blocks, acting as a critique of standardization.

### 3.5 Framed Landscape

The large void turns the distant landscape into a shared view, framing the Guadarrama Mountains and city horizon.

## 5. Light and Environmental Experience

Mirador’s environmental experience is shaped by its large void and stacked massing.

The environmental logic includes:

1. **Central void brings air and light.**  
   The sky plaza opens the mass and creates a large shared outdoor space.

2. **Different facade orientations create variation.**  
   Housing groups experience different sunlight, views, and exposure.

3. **Sky plaza creates elevated outdoor comfort.**  
   The void provides an outdoor communal space above the surrounding district.

4. **Circulation voids prevent complete solidity.**  
   Vertical alleys and red routes perforate the block.

5. **Facade variation gives identity.**  
   Materials and colors help differentiate different housing groups and create social visibility.

The project uses massing and voids to make social housing more open, varied, and communal.

## 6. Spatial Hierarchy

The photos and drawings suggest a clear spatial hierarchy:

### 6.1 City / District Layer

The building sits within the repetitive housing district of Sanchinarro.

### 6.2 Ground Plaza Layer

### 6.3 Housing Group Layer

The tower is composed of multiple stacked housing neighbourhoods.

### 6.4 Circulation Layer

Red circulation routes connect ground, units, and sky plaza.

### 6.5 Sky Plaza Layer

The elevated void becomes the main collective space.

### 6.6 View / Landmark Layer

The large opening frames distant landscape and gives the tower landmark identity.

The hierarchy can be summarized as:

**Sanchinarro District → Ground Access → Stacked Housing Groups → Red Circulation → Sky Plaza → Guadarrama View / Landmark Identity**

## 7. Design Intent Reading

From the exterior image, site plan, and floor plans, Mirador can be interpreted as a project that redefines social housing through vertical public space.

The design intent can be understood in six main ways:

### 7.1 Turning repetition into difference

The building resists standardized housing by creating multiple housing groups and facade identities.

### 7.2 Creating a collective room in the sky

The sky plaza becomes the symbolic and social center of the project.

### 7.3 Making housing a landmark

Social housing is treated as a public architectural reference point for the district.

### 7.4 Creating vertical streets

The circulation paths work like vertical alleys and neighbourhood routes.

### 7.5 Framing distant landscape

The large void transforms the tower into a frame for the city and mountains.

### 7.6 Critiquing the closed block

The project takes the idea of the closed block and turns it vertically, making the courtyard open to the horizon.

---

## 8. Contextual Relationship

Mirador relates to its context in four main ways:

### 8.1 Relationship with Sanchinarro

The project responds to the uniformity of the new residential district by creating variety and identity.

### 8.2 Relationship with Madrid’s housing expansion

The building is part of a social housing program that experimented with new housing models.

### 8.3 Relationship with the landscape

The sky plaza frames views toward the Guadarrama Mountains.

### 8.4 Relationship with residents

The building tries to create identity, diversity, and communal space within social housing.

## 10. Preprocess Output Summary

### Main Visual Character

Mirador Building presents social housing as a vertical superblock with a large sky plaza cut through its mass. Its identity is defined by the central void, patchwork facade, red circulation, stacked mini-neighbourhoods, and framed mountain view.

### Main Atmospheric Quality

The project creates an atmosphere of collective vertical urbanism. It is monumental, social, varied, and intentionally different from the repetitive residential blocks around it.

### Main Design Reading

Mirador uses the lifted courtyard as a new model for social housing. Its key contribution is the transformation of the standard housing block into a vertical neighbourhood with shared sky space, varied housing types, and a landmark identity.
