## 1. Urban Location & Positioning

Mirador Building is located in Sanchinarro, a new residential district in the north of Madrid. The area was developed as part of Madrid’s urban expansion and is characterized by large housing blocks, wide roads, peripheral open spaces, and a repetitive residential fabric.

The project was designed as a response to the monotony of the surrounding six- to eight-storey housing blocks. Instead of following the typical closed urban block with an internal patio, Mirador turns this block condition vertically. The result is a 21-storey residential tower with a large elevated void that works as a semi-public sky plaza and lookout.

The site can be understood through four urban conditions:

1. **Peripheral residential expansion condition**  
   Sanchinarro is located on the northern edge of Madrid and was developed as a large new housing district.

2. **Uniform housing block condition**  
   The surrounding urban fabric is dominated by repetitive residential blocks, producing a sense of monotony.

3. **Urban landmark condition**  
   Mirador stands as a visible reference point within the new district because of its height, color, scale, and large void.

4. **Lifted public space condition**  
   The project lifts the idea of the courtyard into the air, creating a communal space approximately 40 m above ground.

Mirador therefore operates as both social housing and an urban critique. It challenges the repetitive housing block by creating a vertical community with multiple housing types, circulation routes, facade identities, and a shared sky plaza.

The surrounding context reveals the main design problem: how to create social identity, variety, and communal space in a repetitive and peripheral housing district.

Mirador responds by stacking different housing “neighbourhoods” vertically and carving a large communal void through the tower. This void becomes both a collective garden and a visual lookout.

## 4. Sunlight & Shadow Conditions

Madrid has a continental Mediterranean climate with hot summers, cold winters, strong sunlight, and dry conditions. For collective housing in this climate, sunlight, shading, ventilation, and outdoor communal space are important.

Mirador responds to these conditions through its vertical void and block-like massing.

Key sunlight and shadow implications include:

1. **Large elevated void**  
   The central opening brings light and air into the middle of the tower and creates an outdoor communal space.

2. **Stacked massing**  
   The tower is composed of smaller housing blocks stacked together, creating different facade orientations and shadow patterns.

3. **Facade depth and material variation**  
   Different facade materials, colors, and window patterns create visual differentiation between housing groups.

4. **Semi-public sky plaza**  
   The sky plaza provides an outdoor space at height, allowing residents to experience sun, wind, and views beyond the surrounding urban blocks.

5. **Red circulation voids**  
   The circulation system creates vertical “streets” and internal paths that connect different parts of the tower.

The building uses void, circulation, and stacking to create environmental and social variety within a large housing block.

## 5. Views & Vistas

Mirador’s most important view condition is its elevated sky plaza.

### 5.1 Guadarrama Mountain View

The large void frames distant views toward the Guadarrama Mountains.

### 5.2 City View

The tower looks out over Madrid’s northern residential expansion and surrounding districts.

### 5.3 Sky Plaza View

The communal opening provides residents with a shared viewing platform, rather than limiting views to private apartments only.

### 5.4 Neighborhood View

Apartments and circulation areas also face the surrounding Sanchinarro blocks, roads, and open urban fabric.

The view strategy is not only about luxury or private panorama. It transforms view into a collective amenity for social housing residents.

## 6. Design Implications

The site context suggests that Mirador’s design strategy can be interpreted through six main ideas.

### 6.1 Turning the urban block vertical

The project takes the typical housing block and lifts it upward, transforming a courtyard block into a vertical superblock.

### 6.2 Creating a sky plaza

The central courtyard is moved from the ground to the air, creating a semi-public communal space at approximately 40 m above ground.

### 6.3 Breaking housing monotony

The building uses nine housing groups, varied unit types, different facade materials, and changing circulation paths to resist repetition.

### 6.4 Making social housing visible

The project turns social housing into an urban landmark rather than a background residential block.

### 6.5 Creating vertical neighbourhoods

The tower is organized as stacked mini-neighbourhoods, each with its own identity, facade, and unit configuration.

### 6.6 Connecting residents to landscape

The sky plaza frames distant mountain views and gives residents access to a shared landscape experience.

## 7. Site Context Summary

Mirador Building is located in Sanchinarro, Madrid, a peripheral new residential district characterized by repetitive housing blocks and wide roads.

The main site conditions are:

1. **Peripheral expansion district**  
   The project sits in a newly developed northern area of Madrid.

2. **Uniform residential fabric**  
   The surrounding blocks are repetitive and introverted.

3. **Need for communal identity**  
   The district lacks strong collective spaces and visual landmarks.

4. **View potential**  
   The elevated building can frame views toward Madrid and the Guadarrama Mountains.

5. **Social housing diversity**  
   The project seeks to create varied housing types and identities within one building.

Mirador responds by transforming the housing block into a vertical neighbourhood. Its large void, sky plaza, stacked housing groups, red circulation paths, and varied facades create an alternative model for social housing density.

Therefore, Mirador Building can be understood as a high-rise residential precedent that uses lifted courtyard space, vertical neighbourhoods, and unit diversity to challenge repetitive social housing models.
