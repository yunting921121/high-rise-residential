## 1. Purpose of Drawing Preprocess

This drawing preprocess translates architectural drawings and diagrams into textual information for Precedent DNA analysis.

The purpose is to extract:

1. **Architectural vocabulary**  
   Important spatial elements, unit types, central core, private elevator lobby, exoskeleton, terraces, amenity zones, and structural facade components.

2. **Spatial relationship descriptions**  
   How the central core, residence zones, half-floor units, full-floor units, exoskeleton, terraces, and luxury amenities relate to each other.

3. **Overall spatial logic**  
   The design logic behind One Thousand Museum as a luxury tower where structural exoskeleton, unit hierarchy, and panoramic living are integrated.

## 2. Drawing Inventory

### 2.1 Upper Zone Unit Diagram

The upper zone diagram shows a full-floor residence. The plan includes a private elevator lobby, great room, dining area, butler pantry, kitchen, family room, wet bar, bedrooms, master bedroom, closets, master bathroom, media room, laundry, and staff area.

The full-floor unit occupies the entire perimeter around the central core. It is organized as a large luxury residence with panoramic exposure.

This diagram shows that the upper zone is the most exclusive residential area of the tower.

### 2.2 Mid Zone Unit Diagram

The mid zone diagram shows two half-floor residences on one level. Each unit has a private elevator lobby, great room, kitchen, bedrooms, master suite, bathrooms, and staff / laundry zone.

The central core divides the floor into two large units. Each half-floor unit has a strong perimeter relationship and access to views.

This diagram shows a balance between exclusivity and density.

### 2.3 Low Zone Unit Diagram

The low zone diagram also shows half-floor units, but the floor plate is slightly different because of the changing exoskeleton and lower tower geometry.

The lower-zone plan reveals how the exoskeleton influences corner positions, room edges, balconies, and facade geometry.

### 2.4 Exoskeleton Elevation Studies

The elevation studies compare several structural facade concepts, including expressed structure proposals, eroded proposal, and lattice proposal.

These studies show that the tower’s final identity came from the search for a structural exoskeleton that could integrate architecture and engineering.

The exoskeleton is not decoration. It is a load-bearing and lateral-resisting system expressed on the facade.

### 2.5 Exterior Image

The exterior image shows the tower as a slender high-rise with a white curving exoskeleton. The structure wraps around the glass volume and becomes more complex at the base, where it branches into large sculptural supports.

The image confirms the main design logic:

**Structure becomes facade, facade becomes identity.**

## 5. Spatial Relationship Descriptions

### 5.1 Central Core → organizes → Residential Units

The central core contains elevators, stairs, services, and private lobbies, organizing the residential floor plates.

### 5.2 Exoskeleton → supports → Tower Perimeter

The exterior structural frame supports the tower perimeter and resists lateral loads.

### 5.3 Exoskeleton → frees → Interior Floor Plate

By moving major structural support to the exterior, the tower reduces interior columns and creates more open residential layouts.

### 5.4 Half-Floor Units → occupy → Low and Mid Zones

Half-floor residences occupy much of the lower and mid-level tower, balancing privacy, size, and density.

### 5.5 Full-Floor Units → occupy → Upper Zone

Full-floor residences occupy upper levels, gaining panoramic views and increased exclusivity.

### 5.6 Private Elevator Lobby → serves → Residence Unit

Private elevator lobbies create exclusive arrival sequences for each residence.

### 5.7 Great Room → faces → Panoramic View

Great rooms and main living spaces are placed along the facade to take advantage of bay and city views.

### 5.8 Terraces → extend → Residential Living

Terraces and balconies extend the residences outward, creating outdoor living space and view platforms.

### 5.9 Exoskeleton Curvature → creates → Floor Plan Variation

The changing curvature of the exoskeleton creates slight differences in each floor plan.

### 5.10 Branching Base → transfers → Structural Loads

The exoskeleton widens and branches at the base to transfer loads and create a strong sculptural foundation.

### 5.11 Upper Amenity Levels → provide → Shared Luxury Programs

Aquatic center, lounge, event space, and roof-related functions create a vertical amenity system.

### 5.12 Structural Facade → defines → Architectural Identity

The tower’s identity is produced by the fusion of structure, facade, and form.

## 6. Overall Spatial Logic

One Thousand Museum’s spatial logic is based on the integration of structure and luxury residential planning.

The structural sequence can be summarized as:

**Central Core → Exterior Exoskeleton → Rigid Tube → Hurricane-Resistant Tower → Column-Reduced Interior**

The residential sequence can be summarized as:

**Private Elevator Lobby → Residence Unit → Great Room / Bedroom → Terrace / Glass Facade → Bay or City View**

The vertical program sequence can be summarized as:

**Podium and Parking → Low and Mid Half-Floor Units → Upper Full-Floor Units → Penthouse / Amenities / Helipad**

Through this strategy, the project combines:

1. **Expressive structural identity**
2. **Luxury residential exclusivity**
3. **Large open floor plans**
4. **Panoramic views**
5. **Hurricane-resistant engineering**
6. **Iconic skyline presence**
7. **Vertical amenity stacking**

The project demonstrates how a residential tower can use structure as the primary architectural language.

---

## 7. Design Interpretation

The drawing analysis suggests that One Thousand Museum is a project about turning structural necessity into architectural expression.

The most important design interpretation is:

**The exoskeleton becomes the architecture.**

The tower’s white exterior frame is not an applied ornament. It supports the perimeter, resists lateral forces, enables larger interior spans, creates balcony conditions, and defines the visual identity of the building.

The unit diagrams also show a clear hierarchy of residence types. Lower and mid levels contain half-floor residences, while upper levels contain full-floor units and penthouse residences. This creates a vertical gradient of exclusivity.

This makes One Thousand Museum a useful precedent for high-rise residential design where structure, luxury planning, facade identity, and climate engineering are deeply integrated.
