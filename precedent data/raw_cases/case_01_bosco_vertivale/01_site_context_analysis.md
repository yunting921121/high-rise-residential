# 01 Site Context Analysis - Bosco Verticale

* **Building Type**: High-rise Residential
* **Main Theme**: Vertical forest, ecological facade, balcony greenery, urban biodiversity
* **Observation Source**: Google Maps satellite image, project photos, architectural diagrams

## 1. Urban Location & Positioning

Bosco Verticale is located in Milan, Italy, at the edge between the Porta Nuova urban redevelopment district and the older Isola neighborhood. From the satellite image, the project sits in a transitional urban condition: on one side, it connects to traditional low- to mid-rise residential blocks and local commercial streets; on the other side, it faces new public open spaces, parks, pedestrian routes, and high-rise office developments.

The project can therefore be understood as a high-rise residential prototype positioned between two different urban fabrics:

1. **Traditional urban fabric**
   The west and northwest sides are connected to the Isola neighborhood, which contains smaller street blocks, local restaurants, residential buildings, and a denser street pattern.

2. **New urban redevelopment fabric**
   The south and east sides are connected to the Porta Nuova redevelopment area, including large public green spaces, office towers, pedestrian paths, and cultural facilities.

Bosco Verticale does not stand as an isolated tower. It functions as an ecological landmark within a larger urban renewal context. Its vertical greenery visually and environmentally extends the surrounding park system upward into the residential towers.

## 4. Sunlight & Shadow Conditions

Because Milan is located in the northern hemisphere, the southern facade generally receives stronger and longer sunlight, while the northern facade receives less direct sunlight. The eastern side receives morning light, and the western side receives stronger afternoon sunlight.

From the site condition, the east and south sides are more open because they face public green space and larger redevelopment areas. These orientations likely provide better exposure, longer views, and stronger sunlight. The west side is closer to the denser Isola neighborhood fabric and may experience more visual proximity to surrounding buildings, as well as afternoon solar gain.

Bosco Verticale responds to these environmental conditions through its planting strategy. The vegetation is not only decorative; it acts as an environmental filter. The balcony greenery helps reduce solar heat gain, filters views, creates shade, and forms a microclimatic buffer between the residential units and the city.

The towers themselves may also cast shadows onto nearby public spaces and streets, especially during lower sun angles. This makes the relationship between tower height, vegetation density, balcony projection, and surrounding open space important for environmental performance.

---

## 5. Views & Vistas

The project has strong visual relationships with the surrounding public landscape and urban skyline.

The **east and south sides** have major view advantages because they face public green spaces, pedestrian routes, and the Porta Nuova redevelopment area. These sides allow residents to overlook parks, plazas, and the broader urban landscape.

The **west side** faces the Isola neighborhood, giving the residential units a closer relationship with the traditional city fabric. Compared with the open east and south sides, this direction may require more attention to privacy, view filtering, and facade buffering.

The balcony vegetation creates a layered visual condition. Residents do not look directly from the interior to the city; instead, their views pass through trees, shrubs, and balcony planting. This produces a softer threshold between private domestic life and the dense urban environment.

## 6. Design Implications

The site context suggests that Bosco Verticale’s design strategy can be interpreted through four main ideas:

### 6.1 Extending the park vertically

The project is located next to a system of public green spaces. Instead of limiting greenery to the ground plane, Bosco Verticale extends the idea of the park upward through balconies and facade vegetation. The tower becomes a vertical continuation of the surrounding landscape.

### 6.2 Mediating between old and new urban fabrics

The west side connects to the older Isola neighborhood, while the south and east sides connect to the new Porta Nuova development. The building acts as a mediator between these two different urban conditions. Its green facade softens the transition between high-rise development and existing neighborhood scale.

### 6.3 Creating a green buffer for residential life

The balcony planting works as a buffer between the residential units and the dense city. It provides shade, privacy, seasonal change, and visual filtering. This makes high-rise living feel less exposed and more connected to nature.

### 6.4 Transforming the tower into ecological infrastructure

Bosco Verticale is not only a residential building with plants attached to it. The project turns the residential tower into part of the city’s ecological system. Its vegetation contributes to biodiversity, environmental identity, and a new image of high-rise housing.

---

## 7. Site Context Summary

Bosco Verticale is located at the intersection of the traditional Isola neighborhood and the newer Porta Nuova redevelopment district in Milan. The site is surrounded by residential blocks, cultural facilities, public green spaces, pedestrian routes, office towers, and urban plazas.

The project’s main site strategy is to transform the high-rise residential tower into a vertical extension of the surrounding green landscape. Through balcony planting and facade vegetation, the building creates a buffer between private residential life and the dense urban environment.

In this context, the vertical forest strategy responds to three major site conditions:

1. The presence of nearby public parks and open spaces
2. The contrast between old neighborhood fabric and new high-rise development
3. The need to soften the environmental and visual impact of high-density residential towers

Therefore, Bosco Verticale can be understood as a high-rise residential precedent that uses vegetation, balconies, and ecological facade systems to connect housing, urban density, and public green infrastructure.
