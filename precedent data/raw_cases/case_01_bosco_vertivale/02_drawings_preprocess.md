## 1. Purpose of Drawing Preprocess

This drawing preprocess translates architectural drawings and diagrams into textual information that can be used for Precedent DNA analysis.

The goal is not to describe the images visually only, but to extract:

1. **Architectural vocabulary**
   Important spatial elements, environmental systems, and building components.

2. **Spatial relationship descriptions**
   How the elements connect, support, surround, serve, or transform each other.

3. **Overall spatial logic**
   The main design logic behind the organization of tower, balcony, vegetation, unit, core, and environmental systems.

---

## 2. Drawing / Diagram Inventory

### 2.1 Water Supply System Diagram

The water supply system diagram shows that the vertical greenery is supported by a technical irrigation system. The vegetation is not treated as independent decoration, but as part of an integrated building system. Water is distributed vertically through the tower and supplied to planters on multiple floors.

### 2.2 Vegetation System Diagram

The vegetation diagram separates the plant layer from the architectural frame. It shows that trees, shrubs, and plants are distributed across the tower facade and balconies. The vegetation forms an independent but integrated ecological layer attached to the residential tower.

### 2.3 Vertical Forest Composition Diagram

The vertical forest diagram combines the structural tower, balcony slabs, planters, and vegetation into one architectural system. It shows how the building becomes a vertical landscape by stacking planted balconies across multiple residential floors.

### 2.4 Seasonal Facade Diagram

The seasonal facade diagram shows that the facade changes across different seasons. The vegetation layer produces different colors, densities, and degrees of opacity depending on seasonal growth and plant conditions. This suggests that the facade is dynamic rather than fixed.

### 2.5 Typical Residential Floor Plan

The floor plan shows residential units organized around a vertical circulation and service core. The units extend outward toward balconies, and the balconies act as exterior living spaces. The plan also shows that greenery is placed along the perimeter, creating a buffer between interior rooms and the urban environment.

## 5. Spatial Relationship Descriptions

### 5.1 Residential Unit → opens to → Balcony

Each residential unit is connected to an exterior balcony. The balcony extends the private domestic space outward and provides access to light, air, view, and planting.

### 5.2 Balcony → supports → Planter

The balcony is not only an outdoor platform. It also functions as a structural and spatial support for planters. This transforms the balcony into an ecological device.

### 5.3 Planter → contains → Vegetation

The planters contain trees, shrubs, and smaller plants. This allows vegetation to be distributed vertically across the residential facade.

### 5.4 Vegetation → wraps → Tower Facade

The vegetation layer wraps around the tower facade and softens the appearance of the high-rise mass. It produces a living, seasonal, and irregular facade condition.

### 5.5 Irrigation System → supplies → Planter

The irrigation system supplies water to planters across multiple floors. This relationship is essential because the vertical forest depends on technical infrastructure for long-term maintenance.

### 5.6 Vertical Core → serves → Residential Unit

The vertical circulation core serves the residential units by organizing elevators, stairs, service shafts, and vertical movement. It allows the residential units to be efficiently stacked in the tower.

### 5.7 Structural Slab → cantilevers from → Tower Mass

The projecting balcony slabs extend outward from the tower mass. These cantilevered slabs create space for planters and allow vegetation to become part of the facade depth.

### 5.8 Seasonal Planting → transforms → Facade Appearance

The seasonal planting changes the color, density, and visual texture of the building facade. The facade becomes dynamic and changes over time.

### 5.9 Twin Towers → frame → Urban Landscape

The two towers stand as vertical elements within the urban redevelopment district. Together with nearby public green spaces, they create a strong relationship between high-rise housing and urban landscape.

## 6. Overall Spatial Logic

Bosco Verticale transforms the typical high-rise residential tower by turning the balcony into a thick ecological interface.

Instead of treating the facade as a flat surface, the project builds a layered facade system:

1. **Residential interior layer**
   Domestic spaces such as living rooms and bedrooms form the private living area.

2. **Balcony layer**
   Projecting balconies extend the living area outward and create usable outdoor space.

3. **Planter layer**
   Planters are integrated into the balconies and support vegetation.

4. **Vegetation layer**
   Trees and shrubs form a vertical forest around the tower.

5. **Technical maintenance layer**
   Irrigation and water supply systems support the long-term survival of the plants.

The main spatial logic can be summarized as:

**Residential Unit → Balcony → Planter → Vegetation → Urban Environment**

Through this sequence, the residential tower becomes both a living space and an ecological facade system.

---

## 7. Design Interpretation

The drawing analysis suggests that Bosco Verticale is not simply a tower with plants attached to it. The project integrates architecture, landscape, structure, and environmental systems into one design strategy.

The most important design interpretation is that the project changes the role of the balcony. In a typical high-rise residential project, balconies are often secondary outdoor spaces. In Bosco Verticale, the balcony becomes:

* an outdoor living extension
* a planting platform
* a shading device
* a privacy buffer
* a microclimate regulator
* a facade-making element
* a biodiversity support system

This makes the balcony the core spatial element of the project.
