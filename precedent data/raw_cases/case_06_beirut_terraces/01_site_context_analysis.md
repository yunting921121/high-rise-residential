## 1. Urban Location & Positioning

Beirut Terraces is located in Beirut Central District, within the Mina / Minet el-Hosn area near the Mediterranean shoreline. The project occupies a high-value urban location close to Beirut’s waterfront, marina-related developments, luxury residential towers, hotels, commercial blocks, and historically charged urban fragments.

The site can be understood as a dense urban coastal condition. It is not an isolated residential tower; it belongs to a district where Beirut’s reconstruction, luxury development, seafront identity, and urban memory overlap.

The project is positioned between several important urban conditions:

1. **Waterfront and sea-view condition**
   The building is close to the Mediterranean shoreline, allowing apartments to gain sea views and long-distance urban views.

2. **Beirut Central District redevelopment**
   The surrounding area contains luxury residential towers, hotels, commercial programs, and post-war redevelopment projects.

3. **Historical urban memory**
   The project is near the ruined Holiday Inn Beirut, a building strongly associated with the Lebanese Civil War. This creates a contrast between Beirut’s damaged urban history and its contemporary luxury redevelopment.

4. **Dense high-rise residential context**
   Nearby towers such as Platinum Tower create a context of vertical residential development near the waterfront.

Beirut Terraces responds to this condition by creating a tower that is not a sealed glass object. Instead, it uses horizontal slabs, deep terraces, vegetation, and alternating indoor-outdoor zones to create a layered domestic environment in the city.

## 4. Sunlight & Shadow Conditions

Beirut has a Mediterranean climate with strong sun, warm summers, and the need for shading, outdoor comfort, and protection from heat gain. In this context, a high-rise residential tower needs to manage sunlight carefully while still providing views and outdoor living space.

Beirut Terraces responds through its layered slab system:

1. **Deep overhangs**
   The projecting floor plates create shade for terraces and interior spaces.

2. **Setback and projection variation**
   Different floor plates shift in and out, producing varied conditions of exposure, shelter, light, and shadow.

3. **Terrace depth**
   Large terrace zones create a buffer between interior rooms and exterior climate.

4. **Vegetation**
   Planting on terraces further filters sunlight, cools outdoor spaces, and creates privacy.

5. **Thermal mass and horizontal slabs**
   Thick horizontal floor plates help create a more stable thermal and visual environment.

The project does not rely only on mechanical cooling or sealed facades. Its envelope is spatially thick, using terraces and overhangs as passive environmental devices.

## 5. Views & Vistas

The project’s location close to the Beirut shoreline gives it strong view potential. The terraces are arranged to open apartments toward the city, the sea, and the surrounding skyline while maintaining privacy between units.

The project creates several view conditions:

### 5.1 Sea View

Upper floors and north-facing / northwest-facing terraces can gain views toward the Mediterranean Sea, Zaitunay Bay, and Saint George Bay.

### 5.2 City View

Other orientations connect to Beirut Central District, surrounding towers, hotels, and reconstructed urban blocks.

### 5.3 Terrace View

The immediate foreground of many apartments is the terrace itself: shaded outdoor space, planting, and layered slabs frame the view before it reaches the city.

### 5.4 Filtered Private View

Vegetation and slab depth help prevent apartments from being fully exposed to neighboring towers. The view is filtered through outdoor layers rather than being completely open.

This view strategy is important because the building is located in a dense luxury tower context. The terraces must provide openness while also protecting privacy.

## 6. Design Implications

The site context suggests that Beirut Terraces’ design strategy can be interpreted through six main ideas.

### 6.1 Creating outdoor living in a dense city

The project uses terraces to create generous exterior domestic spaces, allowing high-rise residents to live between inside and outside.

### 6.2 Responding to Mediterranean climate

Deep slabs, overhangs, and vegetation create shade and reduce solar exposure while still allowing views and openness.

### 6.3 Creating privacy within density

The project is surrounded by other high-rise developments. Terraces, vegetation, and setbacks create privacy buffers between apartments and neighboring buildings.

### 6.4 Turning the facade into layered domestic space

The building envelope is not flat. It is a sequence of slabs, terraces, glazed interiors, shaded edges, and planted zones.

### 6.5 Expressing Beirut’s layered urban identity

The stacked slabs can be read as a response to the city’s layered history, reconstruction, and vertical growth.

### 6.6 Creating unit individuality through modular variation

The tower uses repeated modular floor types, but their combinations produce non-identical apartments and varied terrace conditions.

## 7. Site Context Summary

Beirut Terraces is located in the Minet el-Hosn area of Beirut Central District, close to the Mediterranean shoreline, Zaitunay Bay, Platinum Tower, and the ruined Holiday Inn Beirut.

The main site conditions are:

1. **Dense high-value waterfront context**
   The project is surrounded by luxury towers, commercial buildings, hotels, and marina-related development.

2. **Mediterranean climate**
   Strong sunlight, heat, and outdoor living culture require shade, terraces, and spatial buffers.

3. **Need for privacy**
   The dense tower context requires careful control of views between neighboring buildings.

4. **Historical and urban layering**
   The site belongs to a city shaped by war, reconstruction, luxury development, and coastal identity.

5. **View potential**
   The proximity to the sea and city skyline gives apartments valuable outward views.

Beirut Terraces responds by transforming the high-rise residential tower into a stack of layered indoor-outdoor living platforms. The terraces create shade, privacy, views, vegetation, and domestic outdoor space within a dense coastal city.

Therefore, Beirut Terraces can be understood as a high-rise residential precedent that uses terrace layering, slab projection, vegetation, and flexible apartment planning to connect high-rise living with climate, views, and urban identity.
