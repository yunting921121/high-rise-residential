## 1. Purpose of Photos and Renders Preprocess

This preprocess translates photos, diagrams, and visual materials into textual descriptions for architectural precedent analysis.

The goal is to extract:

1. **Design vocabulary**
   Visible architectural elements, terrace forms, facade components, vegetation, and spatial atmosphere.

2. **Atmospheric description**
   The visual and spatial atmosphere created by the building’s layered slabs, shaded terraces, white materiality, vegetation, and coastal city context.

3. **Design intent reading**
   The possible design intention behind the building’s stratified expression and indoor-outdoor living system.

4. **Contextual relationship**
   How the building relates to Beirut’s climate, sea views, dense urban fabric, and residential lifestyle.

## 3. Atmospheric Description

Beirut Terraces creates the atmosphere of a vertical Mediterranean neighborhood.

From the exterior photo, the building appears as a stack of white horizontal platforms. The slabs project and recede, creating a strong layered expression. Unlike a smooth glass tower, Beirut Terraces has a deep and inhabitable facade. The exterior is not only a surface; it is a sequence of terraces, shade, outdoor rooms, and planted edges.

The atmosphere can be described through five qualities:

### 3.1 Layered Openness

The building feels open because of its terraces and glazing, but it is not fully exposed. Each layer creates a different balance between openness and enclosure.

### 3.2 Mediterranean Outdoor Living

The terraces suggest an everyday lifestyle where residents can eat, rest, gather, and look outward while remaining shaded from strong sun.

### 3.3 White Horizontal Lightness

The white slabs give the building a bright, lightweight appearance. The horizontal lines emphasize layering and create strong shadow patterns.

### 3.4 Privacy through Depth

Privacy is not achieved through solid walls alone. Instead, privacy is produced by depth: terraces, vegetation, setbacks, and overhangs.

### 3.5 Urban-Coastal Luxury

The project combines dense city living with sea-oriented luxury. The tower’s terraces allow apartments to connect to Beirut’s waterfront and skyline.

## 5. Light and Environmental Experience

The project uses slab depth and terrace layering to control light and heat.

The building’s environmental logic includes:

1. **Overhangs provide shade.**
   Projecting slabs reduce direct sunlight on glazing and terraces.

2. **Terraces create thermal buffers.**
   Outdoor zones sit between interior rooms and the hot exterior climate.

3. **Vegetation cools and filters.**
   Planting provides shade, privacy, and a softer microclimate.

4. **Glazing allows openness.**
   Floor-to-ceiling glass connects interiors to terraces and views.

5. **Layering creates varied exposure.**
   Some spaces are open and exposed; others are sheltered and private.

The facade therefore works as a climate-responsive system rather than a purely visual surface.

## 7. Design Intent Reading

From the photos, floor plans, and diagrams, Beirut Terraces can be interpreted as a project that redefines high-rise living through terrace layering.

The design intent can be understood in six main ways:

### 7.1 Making outdoor space central to high-rise living

The project does not treat balconies as small attachments. Terraces become major living areas and define the whole tower.

### 7.2 Blurring inside and outside

Glazing, terraces, and continuous floor surfaces create a strong relationship between interiors and outdoor spaces.

### 7.3 Creating privacy through layering

Privacy is achieved by spatial depth, planting, slab shifts, and terrace arrangement.

### 7.4 Responding to climate through passive shading

Deep overhangs and slab projections protect apartments from strong sun and reduce heat exposure.

### 7.5 Creating individuality within a high-rise

Different floor plate combinations create different terrace conditions, making each apartment feel unique.

### 7.6 Turning the facade into a vertical neighborhood

The building’s terraces produce a facade where domestic life, outdoor activity, vegetation, and views are visible as part of the tower’s identity.

## 8. Contextual Relationship

Beirut Terraces relates to its context in four main ways:

### 8.1 Relationship with the Mediterranean Sea

The tower uses terraces to frame sea views and connect apartments to the coastal landscape.

### 8.2 Relationship with Beirut’s dense urban fabric

The project provides privacy and outdoor space within a dense high-rise environment.

### 8.3 Relationship with climate

The deep slabs, shade, and vegetation respond to Beirut’s sun and warm climate.

### 8.4 Relationship with Beirut’s layered history

The stacked slab expression can be interpreted as an architectural response to Beirut’s layered urban identity, where history, reconstruction, and contemporary luxury coexist.
