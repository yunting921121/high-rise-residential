## 1. Urban Location & Positioning

Tour Bois-le-Prêtre is located at Porte Pouchet in the 17th arrondissement of Paris, near the northern edge of the city and close to the Boulevard Périphérique.

The project belongs to a post-war social housing context. The original tower was built in the late 1950s and early 1960s as part of a modernist housing strategy that placed high-rise residential buildings near the edge of Paris.

The site can be understood through four main urban conditions:

1. **Peripheral Paris condition**  
   The building is located near the edge of Paris, close to the administrative and urban boundary between Paris, Clichy, and Saint-Ouen.

2. **Ring road infrastructure condition**  
   The nearby Boulevard Périphérique creates traffic noise, air pollution, and infrastructural pressure.

3. **Social housing renewal condition**  
   The tower represents an existing HLM social housing block that was initially considered for demolition but was later transformed.

4. **Urban repair condition**  
   The project is part of a broader effort to improve the Porte Pouchet area through housing renewal, new public spaces, and better neighborhood integration.

Tour Bois-le-Prêtre therefore operates as a transformation project rather than a new-build tower. Its urban value comes from the decision to keep the existing building and improve its living conditions from the outside.

The surrounding context reveals the key problem of the project: how to improve existing social housing without demolition, displacement, or complete reconstruction.

The design response is not to erase the existing building. Instead, the project adds new layers around it: extensions, winter gardens, balconies, and a new facade system. These additions transform the residents’ daily life while keeping the original concrete structure.

## 4. Sunlight & Shadow Conditions

Paris has a temperate climate with moderate sunlight, cold winters, and changing seasonal conditions. For an older social housing tower, thermal comfort, daylight, ventilation, and usable exterior space are important issues.

Before transformation, many apartments were small and had limited environmental comfort. The renovation improved this through external additions.

Key sunlight and shadow implications include:

1. **Winter gardens as climatic buffers**  
   The new winter gardens create intermediate spaces between interior rooms and exterior climate.

2. **Balconies as outdoor extensions**  
   New balconies give residents access to exterior space and daylight.

3. **Improved facade transparency**  
   The new envelope allows more light into apartments and gives residents broader views.

4. **Thermal improvement without demolition**  
   The added exterior layer improves comfort while keeping the existing structure.

5. **Resident-controlled environmental edge**  
   The winter gardens allow residents to control openness, curtains, ventilation, and use according to season.

The project treats the facade as an inhabitable environmental layer rather than a thin enclosure.

## 5. Views & Vistas

The transformation improves both interior and exterior view conditions.

### 5.1 City-edge View

The tower’s height allows views over the northern edge of Paris and the surrounding urban infrastructure.

### 5.2 Neighborhood View

Residents look toward nearby residential blocks, local streets, cemetery landscape, and Porte Pouchet redevelopment areas.

### 5.3 Winter Garden View

The immediate view from the apartment is changed by the new winter garden. Instead of looking directly from a small interior room to the outside, residents now look through an additional living layer.

### 5.4 Balcony View

Balconies create direct outdoor viewpoints for residents and improve the everyday relationship between the apartment and the city.

The view strategy is important because the project increases the quality of existing homes without relocating residents. The new facade layer changes the perception of space, light, and outlook.

## 6. Design Implications

The site context suggests that Tour Bois-le-Prêtre’s design strategy can be interpreted through six main ideas.

### 6.1 Do not demolish

The project challenges demolition-based housing renewal. It demonstrates that existing housing blocks can be transformed through addition rather than replacement.

### 6.2 Add instead of subtract

Instead of reducing or stripping the building, the project adds new space, balconies, winter gardens, and facade depth.

### 6.3 Improve social housing from the outside

The main intervention is an external self-supporting structure that allows apartments to expand without destroying the existing building.

### 6.4 Keep residents in place

The transformation strategy supports continuity of residence and reduces displacement.

### 6.5 Increase living quality

The project improves daylight, views, thermal comfort, outdoor space, and apartment generosity.

### 6.6 Make renovation an environmental strategy

By preserving the existing structure, the project reduces demolition waste and embodied carbon compared with replacement.

## 7. Site Context Summary

Tour Bois-le-Prêtre is located at Porte Pouchet, Paris 17e, near the Boulevard Périphérique and the northern edge of Paris.

The main site conditions are:

1. **Existing social housing tower**  
   The project begins with a post-war high-rise housing block.

2. **Peripheral infrastructure context**  
   The site is close to major roads and the edge of Paris.

3. **Demolition pressure**  
   The tower was part of a broader context in which many older social housing blocks were considered for demolition.

4. **Need for improved habitability**  
   Apartments required more space, better daylight, better thermal comfort, and better outdoor access.

5. **Need for social continuity**  
   The project needed to improve housing without removing the residents from their homes.

Tour Bois-le-Prêtre responds by transforming the existing tower through external additions. Winter gardens, balconies, new facade layers, and extended floor plates create a new living environment around the original structure.

Therefore, Tour Bois-le-Prêtre can be understood as a high-rise residential precedent that uses addition, reuse, and inhabited facade depth to transform social housing without demolition.
