## 1. Purpose of Drawing Preprocess

This drawing preprocess translates architectural drawings and diagrams into textual information for Precedent DNA analysis.

The purpose is to extract:

1. **Architectural vocabulary**  
   Important spatial elements, apartment units, central core, terraces, cantilevers, setbacks, and unit variation systems.

2. **Spatial relationship descriptions**  
   How apartment units, rooms, terraces, core, floor plates, stacked volumes, and facade offsets relate to each other.

3. **Overall spatial logic**  
   The design logic behind 56 Leonard Street as a tower made of individualized apartments stacked around a central core.

---

## 2. Drawing Inventory

### 2.1 Unit Distribution Diagram

The unit distribution diagram shows several apartment types arranged vertically through the tower. It includes different unit sizes, such as one-bedroom, two-bedroom, three-bedroom, four-bedroom, and larger full-floor or near-full-floor apartments.

The diagram indicates that the building does not use one identical typical floor. Instead, different apartment types are grouped in vertical zones. These zones create changes in floor plate shape, unit size, terrace position, and facade expression.

This diagram is important because it shows that unit variation is not only a marketing feature. It is the basis of the tower’s architectural form.

### 2.2 Residential Floor Plans

The floor plan drawings show apartments arranged around a compact central core. The core contains elevators, stairs, and service areas, while apartments occupy the perimeter.

The plans reveal several repeated spatial elements:

- Central elevator / stair core
- Apartment entries
- Living rooms
- Bedrooms
- Kitchens
- Bathrooms
- Terraces
- Setback edges
- Structural columns
- Floor-to-ceiling facade zones

The plans also show that different floors have different shapes. Some floors are more compact, while others project or set back in certain directions. This creates varied apartment layouts and exterior spaces.

### 2.3 Exterior Image

The exterior image shows the built consequence of the plan logic. Floors shift outward and inward, creating the appearance of stacked blocks or houses. Balconies and terraces are formed by these offsets.

The tower does not read as a smooth extruded volume. It reads as a vertical stack of individual dwellings.

## 5. Spatial Relationship Descriptions

### 5.1 Central Core → organizes → Apartment Units

The compact central core organizes elevators, stairs, services, and vertical circulation, while apartments are arranged around the perimeter.

### 5.2 Apartment Units → occupy → Perimeter

The apartment units occupy the edge of the floor plate to maximize daylight, views, and access to terraces.

### 5.3 Unit Types → stack into → Vertical Zones

Different unit types are grouped vertically, creating zones of different floor plate sizes and apartment configurations.

### 5.4 Floor Plate Offsets → create → Private Terraces

Setbacks and cantilevers generate outdoor terraces and balconies for individual residences.

### 5.5 Cantilevered Slabs → project from → Tower Core

Some floor plates extend outward beyond the typical tower outline, creating dramatic cantilevered conditions.

### 5.6 Apartment Variation → forms → Pixelated Facade

The variation of apartments, rooms, terraces, and setbacks creates the tower’s pixelated exterior appearance.

### 5.7 Living Rooms → face → City Views

Living areas are generally located along the facade to capture panoramic views of Lower Manhattan and surrounding rivers.

### 5.8 Floor-to-Ceiling Windows → connect → Interior and Skyline

Large glazed openings connect apartment interiors to exterior views and daylight.

### 5.9 Private Terraces → extend → Domestic Life

Terraces and balconies extend the apartments outward, creating outdoor rooms in the sky.

### 5.10 Tower Base → connects to → Street and Public Artwork

The base connects the residential tower to the street and includes a public artwork condition by Anish Kapoor.

### 5.11 Upper Penthouses → crown → Tower

Larger upper residences and dramatic setbacks create the tower’s distinctive top profile.

### 5.12 Stacked Units → challenge → Standard Tower Repetition

The stacking of unique units challenges the anonymity and repetition of standard high-rise residential towers.

## 6. Overall Spatial Logic

56 Leonard Street’s spatial logic is based on stacking individualized homes around a central core.

The residential sequence can be summarized as:

**Central Core → Apartment Unit → Living Space → Terrace / Balcony → City View**

The massing sequence can be summarized as:

**Room Module → Apartment Unit → Offset Floor Plate → Pixelated Tower → Skyline Landmark**

The typological sequence can be summarized as:

**Standard Residential Tower → Unit Differentiation → Stacked Houses → Individualized High-rise**

Through this strategy, 56 Leonard Street combines:

1. **High-rise residential density**
2. **Luxury apartment variation**
3. **Private outdoor space**
4. **Centralized vertical circulation**
5. **Cantilevered structural expression**
6. **Pixelated skyline identity**
7. **Domestic individuality within a tall tower**

The project demonstrates how a residential high-rise can be designed from apartment-level variation rather than from a uniform tower envelope.

---

## 7. Design Interpretation

The drawing analysis suggests that 56 Leonard Street is a project about reversing the anonymity of the high-rise tower.

The most important design interpretation is:

**The apartment becomes the pixel of the tower.**

Each apartment is treated as part of a larger vertical composition. The floor plans show that the building is not a smooth stack of identical floors. Instead, it is composed through the combination of units, rooms, terraces, and setbacks.

The tower’s exterior is therefore not an applied facade. It is the visible result of apartment variation.

This makes 56 Leonard Street a useful precedent for high-rise housing where the goal is to combine density, individuality, outdoor space, and skyline identity.
