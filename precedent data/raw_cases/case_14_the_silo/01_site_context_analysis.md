## 1. Urban Location & Positioning

The Silo is located in Nordhavn, Copenhagen’s North Harbour district. Nordhavn is a former industrial harbor area currently being transformed into a new urban district with housing, offices, public spaces, waterfront promenades, cultural functions, and adaptive reuse projects.

The project occupies a significant position within this transformation because the original silo was one of the largest industrial structures in the area. Instead of demolishing the old grain silo, the project converts it into a residential building with public functions at the top and bottom levels.

The site can be understood through four urban conditions:

1. **Post-industrial harbor condition**  
   The Silo belongs to a former working harbor landscape of silos, warehouses, docks, quays, and industrial infrastructure.

2. **Waterfront redevelopment condition**  
   Nordhavn is being transformed into a new mixed-use waterfront district. The building becomes one of the markers of this transition.

3. **Industrial heritage condition**  
   The project preserves the memory of the old grain silo by keeping the raw concrete structure and converting it into new domestic spaces.

4. **Urban landmark condition**  
   Because of its height, metallic faceted facade, and waterfront position, The Silo becomes a visual reference point in the new district.

The Silo therefore functions as both a residential tower and an urban memory device. It shows how existing industrial structures can be reused rather than erased in new waterfront development.

## 4. Sunlight & Shadow Conditions

Copenhagen has a northern European climate with low winter sun angles, long summer daylight, wind exposure, cold seasons, and strong seasonal variation. For a waterfront residential building, access to daylight, insulation, wind protection, and views are important.

The Silo responds to these conditions through several design strategies:

1. **Large floor-to-ceiling windows**  
   The new residential units use large windows to capture daylight and harbor views.

2. **Balconies and facade recesses**  
   The facade creates balcony-like exterior spaces and deep window conditions that mediate between interior rooms and the exposed harbor climate.

3. **New insulated facade layer**  
   The old concrete structure is wrapped with a new exterior layer that acts as a climate screen.

4. **Retained concrete mass**  
   The existing silo structure gives the building thermal and material depth, while preserving industrial character.

5. **Rooftop public level**  
   The roof level takes advantage of sunlight, sky exposure, and panoramic views.

The facade is therefore not only a new visual skin. It is also an environmental mediator between the raw concrete silo and the contemporary residential interior.

## 5. Views & Vistas

The Silo’s waterfront location and height create several important view conditions:

### 5.1 Harbor View

Apartments and public upper levels can look toward Nordhavn’s basins, quays, boats, and waterfront landscape.

### 5.2 City View

Upper floors can look back toward Copenhagen’s city skyline and surrounding new development.

### 5.3 Øresund / Coastal View

The building’s height and harbor location allow long-distance views toward the coast and open water.

### 5.4 Public Viewpoint

The rooftop restaurant and viewing platform allow non-residents to access the tower’s elevated view, turning the former silo into a public destination.

The view strategy is important because the building does not privatize all of its height. Instead, it shares the top level with the public, creating a civic dimension within an adaptive reuse residential tower.

## 6. Design Implications

The site context suggests that The Silo’s design strategy can be interpreted through six main ideas.

### 6.1 Reusing industrial heritage

The project keeps the existing grain silo instead of demolishing it, preserving Nordhavn’s industrial memory.

### 6.2 Transforming infrastructure into housing

A building originally designed for grain storage is converted into unique apartments with varied interior dimensions and spatial conditions.

### 6.3 Adding a new climate overcoat

The old concrete structure is wrapped in a new faceted exterior facade that improves climate performance and creates a new architectural identity.

### 6.4 Making private housing partly public

The top and lower levels contain public functions, allowing the building to contribute to the wider neighborhood.

### 6.5 Creating a waterfront landmark

The height, metallic facade, and harbor position make The Silo a visual anchor in Nordhavn’s redevelopment.

### 6.6 Preserving raw interior character

The apartments retain traces of the original concrete structure, allowing residents to live within the material memory of the silo.

## 7. Site Context Summary

The Silo is located in Copenhagen’s Nordhavn, a former industrial harbor district undergoing transformation into a new mixed-use urban neighborhood.

The main site conditions are:

1. **Former industrial harbor context**  
   The building was originally a grain silo and belongs to Nordhavn’s working harbor history.

2. **Waterfront redevelopment**  
   The project participates in the transformation of Nordhavn into a contemporary residential and mixed-use district.

3. **Need for adaptive reuse**  
   The project preserves an existing structure instead of replacing it with a new tower.

4. **Need for climate upgrade**  
   The raw concrete silo required a new exterior envelope to meet contemporary comfort and energy standards.

5. **Public landmark opportunity**  
   The building’s height and location make it suitable for public viewing and civic functions.

The Silo responds by transforming a former grain storage structure into a residential and public landmark. It preserves the industrial concrete core, adds a new galvanized steel facade, creates unique apartments, and gives the public access to the top and lower levels.

Therefore, The Silo can be understood as a high-rise residential precedent that uses adaptive reuse, industrial memory, public access, and facade transformation to create a new model of waterfront living.
