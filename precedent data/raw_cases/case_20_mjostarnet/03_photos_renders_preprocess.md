## 1. Purpose of Photos and Renders Preprocess

This preprocess translates photos and visual materials into textual descriptions for architectural precedent analysis.

The goal is to extract:

1. **Design vocabulary**  
   Visible architectural elements, timber facade, exposed frame, lakefront context, residential balconies, and public program components.

2. **Atmospheric description**  
   The visual and spatial atmosphere created by the timber tower, natural landscape, lakefront setting, and small-town context.

3. **Design intent reading**  
   The possible design intention behind using mass timber as the primary architectural and structural identity.

4. **Contextual relationship**  
   How the building relates to Brumunddal, Lake Mjøsa, Norwegian timber culture, and sustainable tall-building innovation.

## 3. Atmospheric Description

Mjøstårnet creates the atmosphere of a warm timber landmark standing in a northern lake landscape.

From the exterior photo, the tower rises above a green park-like setting, with forests and hills in the background. Its timber facade gives the building a warmer and softer appearance than a conventional glass or concrete tower. The exposed structure at the top makes the construction logic visible and reinforces its identity as a timber building.

The atmosphere can be described through five qualities:

### 3.1 Regional Timber Landmark

The tower is a symbol of the local timber industry and Norway’s wood-building ambitions.

### 3.2 Small-Town Verticality

Because Brumunddal is not a dense high-rise city, the tower appears as a strong landmark in the landscape.

### 3.3 Lakefront Warmth

The timber material and lakefront setting create a softer and more natural high-rise atmosphere.

### 3.4 Mixed-use Civic Presence

The public bath, hotel, restaurant, offices, and viewing areas make the building part of public life.

### 3.5 Structural Honesty

The exposed timber crown and visible wood construction communicate the building’s structural identity.

## 5. Light and Environmental Experience

The building’s environmental experience is shaped by its northern climate, lakefront exposure, and timber construction.

The environmental logic includes:

1. **Timber stores carbon.**  
   The use of engineered wood contributes to the building’s low-carbon identity.

2. **Compact vertical form reduces land use.**  
   The mixed-use tower stacks many programs on a relatively small footprint.

3. **Balconies create outdoor thresholds.**  
   Residential balconies allow residents to experience lake views and fresh air.

4. **Large windows bring daylight and views.**  
   Perimeter rooms connect to the lake and landscape.

5. **Public programs extend use beyond residents.**  
   Public bath and viewing areas make the tower a civic destination.

6. **Cold-climate detailing is essential.**  
   Timber structure, envelope, moisture protection, acoustics, and fire safety must be carefully coordinated.

The project’s environmental performance is therefore both material-based and program-based.

The building stands near Lake Mjøsa, with surrounding hills, forests, and open green space.

### 6.3 Office / Hotel Layer

## 7. Design Intent Reading

From the exterior photo and plans, Mjøstårnet can be interpreted as a project that demonstrates mass timber’s capacity for tall, mixed-use construction.

The design intent can be understood in six main ways:

### 7.1 Proving tall timber feasibility

The project demonstrates that engineered wood can be used for an 18-storey mixed-use building.

### 7.2 Connecting architecture to local resources

The timber structure connects the building to Norway’s forestry and wood industry.

### 7.3 Creating a low-carbon landmark

The tower becomes a symbol of sustainable construction and the green shift.

### 7.4 Combining public and private programs

The tower includes public bath, restaurant, hotel, offices, residences, and viewing spaces.

### 7.5 Giving residents lakefront quality

Apartments use height, balconies, and windows to connect to Lake Mjøsa.

### 7.6 Making structure part of identity

The exposed crown and timber facade make the structural material visible and recognizable.

### 9.3 Possible Design Issues

- Tall buildings usually rely on concrete and steel, increasing embodied carbon.
- Small towns may lack strong vertical landmarks but need careful contextual integration.
- Timber high-rises require fire safety, moisture protection, acoustics, and structural stability.
- Mixed-use towers must separate public, hotel, office, and residential circulation.
- Cold-climate balconies and timber facades require careful detailing.

### 9.5 Possible Design Strategies

- Use glulam as the main high-rise structural frame.
- Use CLT floors and walls for repeated stacked levels.
- Stack public, hotel, office, and residential programs vertically.
- Use balconies and windows to connect residences to lake views.
- Include public functions such as bath, restaurant, and viewing terrace.
- Express timber structure at the crown as architectural identity.
- Connect material choice to local timber industry.
- Use timber as both structural system and sustainability narrative.

## 10. Preprocess Output Summary

### Main Visual Character

Mjøstårnet presents a mixed-use high-rise as a warm timber landmark beside Lake Mjøsa. Its identity is defined by glulam structure, timber facade, residential balconies, public base, and exposed timber crown.

### Main Atmospheric Quality

The project creates an atmosphere of regional timber innovation. It feels civic, natural, experimental, and rooted in the lakefront landscape.

### Main Design Reading

Mjøstårnet uses mass timber construction to transform the high-rise into a low-carbon regional landmark. Its key contribution is the integration of glulam structure, CLT floors, mixed-use programming, lakefront living, and public amenities.
