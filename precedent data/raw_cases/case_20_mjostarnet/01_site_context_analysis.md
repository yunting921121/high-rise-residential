## 1. Urban Location & Positioning

Mjøstårnet is located in Brumunddal, Norway, near the shore of Lake Mjøsa. The project stands in a relatively small town rather than a dense metropolitan high-rise district.

This location is important because the building is not only a tower. It is also a regional symbol for Norway’s timber industry, local resources, and sustainable construction. By placing an 18-storey timber building beside Lake Mjøsa, the project turns a local industrial and landscape setting into an international demonstration of tall wood construction.

The site can be understood through four main urban conditions:

1. **Lakefront landmark condition**  
   The tower stands near Lake Mjøsa and becomes a visible marker within the landscape.

2. **Small-town high-rise condition**  
   The building is much taller than its surrounding context, making it a strong vertical reference in Brumunddal.

3. **Timber industry context**  
   The region has a strong relationship with forestry and wood construction. The project uses this context as part of its identity.

4. **Mixed-use civic condition**  
   The tower combines private housing with hotel, offices, restaurant, public bath, conference areas, and viewing spaces, giving it a broader public role.

Mjøstårnet therefore operates as a hybrid between tower, local landmark, timber technology demonstrator, and public facility.

| Context Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Lakefront Context** | The building benefits from open views over Lake Mjøsa and the surrounding landscape. |
| **Small Town Context** | Brumunddal’s urban scale makes the tower highly visible and symbolic. |
| **Public Recreation Context** | The adjoining swimming hall and hotel-related programs make the building part of a public leisure environment. |
| **Timber Industry Context** | The project demonstrates the potential of engineered timber in a region closely associated with forest resources and timber production. |

The surrounding context reveals the main design tension: how can a tall building be inserted into a small-town lakefront setting without becoming an anonymous imported tower type?

Mjøstårnet responds by using timber as both structure and identity. The building’s glulam structure, CLT components, and timber cladding connect the high-rise typology to local material culture.

## 4. Sunlight & Shadow Conditions

Brumunddal has a cold northern climate with long winters, low winter sun angles, snow, wind, and strong seasonal variation. High-rise housing in this climate requires daylight access, thermal comfort, insulated envelopes, wind protection, and usable balconies.

Mjøstårnet responds through several strategies:

1. **Lake-oriented residential units**  
   Residential floors are arranged so apartments can access daylight and views toward Lake Mjøsa.

2. **Balconies for upper residences**  
   Apartments include balconies that provide outdoor space and lake views, although use will vary seasonally.

3. **Timber envelope warmth**  
   Timber materials create a warmer visual and tactile identity in a cold climate.

4. **Compact tower form**  
   The tower has a compact footprint, helping reduce exposed surface area compared with a sprawling form.

5. **Top-level public view areas**  
   Upper levels take advantage of height, daylight, and panoramic landscape views.

Because the building is a tall timber structure in a cold climate, environmental performance depends on the coordination of structure, facade, insulation, fire protection, moisture control, and detailing.

## 5. Views & Vistas

Mjøstårnet’s most important contextual value is its relationship to Lake Mjøsa and the surrounding Norwegian landscape.

### 5.1 Lake View

Residential units, hotel rooms, and upper public spaces can look toward Lake Mjøsa.

### 5.2 Town View

The tower overlooks Brumunddal, making residents and visitors aware of the relationship between town and lake.

### 5.3 Regional Landscape View

Upper levels provide views toward hills, forests, and the wider landscape surrounding the lake.

### 5.4 Public Viewing Experience

The public viewing terrace / upper-level visitor spaces allow the tower’s views to be shared beyond private residents and hotel guests.

The view strategy is therefore both private and public. Private apartments benefit from the landscape, while public programs also allow visitors to experience the height of the timber tower.

## 6. Design Implications

The site context suggests that Mjøstårnet’s design strategy can be interpreted through six main ideas.

### 6.1 Turning timber into a high-rise identity

The project uses engineered timber not only as a material choice but as the conceptual identity of the tower.

### 6.2 Linking local resources and global innovation

The building connects local forestry and timber manufacturing to an international tall-building discussion.

### 6.3 Creating a mixed-use regional landmark

The building combines housing, hotel, offices, restaurant, public bath, and viewing spaces, making it more than a private residential tower.

### 6.4 Demonstrating low-carbon construction potential

The timber structure acts as a prototype for reducing dependence on conventional steel and concrete high-rise systems.

### 6.5 Providing lakefront residential quality

Residential units use height to gain lake views, balconies, and a relationship with the surrounding landscape.

### 6.6 Making the tower a public symbol

Because of its height and material, the building becomes a symbol of Brumunddal and Norway’s green-shift ambitions.

## 7. Site Context Summary

Mjøstårnet is located in Brumunddal, Norway, beside Lake Mjøsa. It is a tall mixed-use timber building in a small-town lakefront setting.

The main site conditions are:

1. **Lakefront visibility**  
   The tower is highly visible beside Lake Mjøsa.

2. **Small-town contrast**  
   The building’s height makes it a landmark within a modest urban context.

3. **Timber industry identity**  
   The project connects to local timber production and Norwegian forest culture.

4. **Mixed-use public role**  
   The tower combines private residences with hotel, offices, restaurant, public bath, and viewing spaces.

5. **Cold-climate performance**  
   The building must respond to winter climate, wind, snow, insulation, moisture, and fire safety.

Mjøstårnet responds by using glulam, CLT, timber cladding, a mixed-use program, and lake-oriented residential spaces to create a high-rise prototype grounded in local material culture.

Therefore, Mjøstårnet can be understood as a high-rise residential precedent that uses timber structure and mixed-use programming to connect local resources, low-carbon construction, public amenities, and lakefront living.
