## 1. Purpose of Drawing Preprocess

This drawing preprocess translates architectural drawings into textual information for Precedent DNA analysis.

The purpose is to extract:

1. **Architectural vocabulary**  
   Important spatial elements, apartment layouts, hotel / office / public bath programs, timber structure, balconies, cores, and mixed-use components.

2. **Spatial relationship descriptions**  
   How residential units, hotel functions, offices, public bath, glulam structure, CLT floors, circulation cores, balconies, and lake views relate to each other.

3. **Overall spatial logic**  
   The design logic behind Mjøstårnet as a mixed-use timber tower where a mass timber structural system supports residential, hotel, office, and public programs.

- Hotel or public service functions

The exterior photo shows a timber-clad tower rising beside a green park-like lakefront landscape. The tower has a warm wooden facade, balconies, and an exposed structural crown / frame at the top.

The image confirms the key design identity: a tall mixed-use building presented as a timber landmark in a natural and small-town setting.

## 5. Spatial Relationship Descriptions

### 5.1 Glulam Frame → supports → Mixed-use Tower

The glulam structural frame carries the tower and allows timber to become the primary high-rise structural identity.

### 5.2 CLT Floors → form → Repeated Residential and Hotel Levels

CLT floor systems create stacked levels for hotel rooms, apartments, and other functions.

### 5.3 Central Corridor → organizes → Apartment Units

The apartment floor is organized around a central corridor, with units arranged along the perimeter.

### 5.4 Apartment Units → face → Lake and Landscape Views

Perimeter apartment rooms and balconies connect residents to Lake Mjøsa and surrounding landscape.

### 5.5 Balconies → extend → Residential Units

Balconies provide private outdoor space and enhance the relationship between apartments and lake views.

### 5.6 Public Bath → anchors → Ground-Level Program

The large public bath gives the building a civic and recreational base.

### 5.7 Hotel and Offices → occupy → Middle Program Zones

Hotel and office functions create a mixed-use vertical sequence between the public base and residential upper levels.

### 5.8 Upper Residential Floors → gain → Panoramic Views

Apartments on upper levels benefit from height, lake views, and landscape exposure.

### 5.9 Public Viewing Terrace → shares → Tower Height

The upper-level viewing terrace allows non-residents to experience the height and view of the timber tower.

### 5.10 Timber Crown → expresses → Structural Identity

The exposed timber frame at the top makes the structural system visible as architectural expression.

### 5.11 Local Timber → connects to → Regional Material Culture

The use of engineered wood connects the tower to Norway’s forestry and timber production context.

### 5.12 Mixed-use Stack → combines → Private and Public Programs

The building combines apartments, hotel, offices, restaurant, conference, public bath, and viewing areas in one vertical structure.

## 6. Overall Spatial Logic

Mjøstårnet’s spatial logic is based on stacking mixed-use programs within a timber structural frame.

The structural sequence can be summarized as:

**Concrete Foundation → Glulam Frame → CLT Floors and Walls → Timber-Clad Envelope → Exposed Timber Crown**

The program sequence can be summarized as:

**Public Entrance / Restaurant / Bath → Conference / Services → Offices → Hotel → Apartments → Viewing Terrace**

The residential sequence can be summarized as:

**Central Corridor → Apartment Unit → Living Room / Bedroom → Balcony / Window → Lake View**

Through this strategy, the building combines:

1. **Tall timber construction**
2. **Mixed-use vertical programming**
3. **Lakefront residential living**
4. **Public recreation and amenities**
5. **Regional timber identity**
6. **Low-carbon building prototype**
7. **Structural expression**

The project demonstrates how mass timber can be used to create a multi-program high-rise in a small-town lakefront context.

---

## 7. Design Interpretation

The drawing analysis suggests that Mjøstårnet is a project about proving that timber can support a tall mixed-use building.

The most important design interpretation is:

**The timber frame becomes the proof of concept.**

The building’s residential planning is relatively conventional: units are arranged around corridors with perimeter views and balconies. However, the tower’s significance lies in its structural and material system.

The project shows that high-rise residential and mixed-use programs can be organized within a glulam and CLT structure. The use of local timber makes the tower both a technical demonstration and a regional identity project.
