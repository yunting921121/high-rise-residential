## 1. Urban Location & Positioning

Vancouver House is located at the north end of the Granville Bridge, where the bridge and its ramps enter Downtown Vancouver. The project occupies a highly constrained urban site shaped by traffic infrastructure, bridge setbacks, irregular parcels, and the transition between the downtown grid and False Creek waterfront.

The site can be understood as an infrastructure-edge condition rather than a typical clean urban block. The surrounding bridge ramps and roads divide the area into leftover triangular parcels. Instead of avoiding these difficult fragments, the project transforms them into an urban design opportunity.

The project is positioned between several urban conditions:

1. **Downtown Vancouver high-rise fabric**
   The tower contributes to Vancouver’s dense downtown residential skyline.

2. **Granville Bridge infrastructure**
   The site is directly shaped by the bridge, traffic ramps, setback requirements, noise, visual exposure, and underside spaces.

3. **False Creek waterfront context**
   The project is close to False Creek, waterfront views, and open urban landscape.

4. **Beach District mixed-use development**
   The base of the project connects with smaller triangular podium buildings, retail spaces, public art, and pedestrian-oriented urban spaces.

Vancouver House therefore functions as both a residential tower and an urban repair project. It turns leftover infrastructure space into a new mixed-use urban village.

## 4. Sunlight & Shadow Conditions

The tower form is shaped partly by the need to avoid excessive shadow impact on nearby public space and park conditions. Because the lower site is constrained by bridge setbacks, the tower begins with a narrow triangular footprint. As it rises above the bridge influence zone, the floor plate expands outward.

This massing strategy creates several environmental implications:

1. **Small lower footprint**
   The tower’s base is narrow due to the bridge setback and site constraints.

2. **Gradual upper expansion**
   Higher floors expand into a more efficient rectangular floor plate once the building clears the bridge constraint zone.

3. **Reduced lower-level impact**
   The narrow base helps open public space at the ground level.

4. **Daylight protection**
   The massing responds to neighboring public space and shadow concerns.

5. **Varied exposure by height**
   Lower units are closer to traffic infrastructure, while upper units gain more light, view, and distance from the bridge.

The building’s sculptural form is therefore not only formal expression. It is also a response to setbacks, daylight, traffic exposure, and real-estate optimization.

## 5. Views & Vistas

Vancouver House benefits from its height and position near False Creek and the downtown skyline. The tower offers several view conditions:

### 5.1 Water View

Higher units can gain views toward False Creek, the waterfront, and Vancouver’s broader landscape.

### 5.2 City View

Units facing downtown engage with Vancouver’s dense urban skyline and neighboring towers.

### 5.3 Bridge / Infrastructure View

Lower floors and podium spaces relate closely to the Granville Bridge and traffic infrastructure.

### 5.4 Skyline Landmark View

The building is also viewed from the bridge and surrounding city. Its twisting tower form makes it a visible marker at the city entrance.

The project’s view logic changes vertically. Lower levels are shaped by infrastructure proximity, while upper levels gain broader water, mountain, and city views.

## 6. Design Implications

The site context suggests that Vancouver House’s design strategy can be interpreted through six main ideas.

### 6.1 Turning a residual site into a high-rise opportunity

The project uses a difficult triangular site shaped by bridge infrastructure as the starting point for architectural form.

### 6.2 Responding to bridge setbacks

The narrow triangular base is a direct response to required distance from the Granville Bridge and traffic zone.

### 6.3 Expanding above the constraint zone

Once the tower rises above the bridge setback zone, the floor plate gradually expands into a more efficient residential shape.

### 6.4 Creating a mixed-use urban village under infrastructure

The project activates leftover spaces below and around the bridge with retail, public art, restaurants, and pedestrian-oriented uses.

### 6.5 Transforming infrastructure into urban identity

Instead of hiding the bridge, the project treats it as the key generator of form and public space.

### 6.6 Producing a new version of Vancouverism

The project evolves the typical Vancouver tower-and-podium model by replacing the standard podium with a bridge-edge urban village and a twisting tower.

## 7. Site Context Summary

Vancouver House is located at the north end of the Granville Bridge in Downtown Vancouver. The site is defined by leftover triangular parcels, bridge ramps, traffic infrastructure, setback restrictions, and proximity to False Creek.

The main site conditions are:

1. **Infrastructure constraint**
   The bridge and its ramps cut the site into difficult triangular fragments.

2. **Setback restriction**
   Required distance from the bridge limits the tower’s lower footprint.

3. **Urban gateway condition**
   The tower is highly visible when entering downtown from Granville Bridge.

4. **Waterfront proximity**
   The site has potential views toward False Creek and Vancouver’s landscape.

5. **Ground-level urban repair**
   The project must activate residual spaces under and around the bridge.

Vancouver House responds by transforming a tiny triangular lower footprint into an expanding tower that becomes rectangular at higher levels. At the same time, the project creates a mixed-use urban village at the base, turning leftover infrastructure space into an active public realm.

Therefore, Vancouver House can be understood as a high-rise residential precedent that uses infrastructure constraint, geometric transformation, and mixed-use ground activation to create a new urban tower typology.
