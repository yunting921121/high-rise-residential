## 1. Purpose of Drawing Preprocess

This drawing preprocess translates architectural drawings and diagrams into textual information for Precedent DNA analysis.

The purpose is to extract:

1. **Architectural vocabulary**
   Important spatial elements, residential components, podium components, bridge-related conditions, and tower form elements.

2. **Spatial relationship descriptions**
   How the tower, triangular base, upper floor plate, residential units, podium village, Granville Bridge, and urban spaces relate to each other.

3. **Overall spatial logic**
   The design logic behind Vancouver House as a tower that grows from a constrained triangular base into a more efficient residential volume.

---

## 2. Drawing Inventory

### 2.1 Residential Unit Plan

The residential unit plan shows a high-level apartment layout with living, dining, kitchen, bedrooms, library, bathrooms, entry, balcony / outdoor living space, and facade edges.

The unit is organized to maximize views and usable interior space. The plan suggests a strong relationship between interior living areas and exterior balcony / facade conditions.

### 2.2 Urban Axonometric Drawing

The axonometric drawing shows the tower rising above a group of smaller triangular and low-rise buildings near the Granville Bridge. The drawing reveals the relationship between the high-rise tower, bridge infrastructure, podium buildings, and surrounding urban blocks.

The tower appears to grow from a constrained base and expand upward. The lower urban village buildings occupy residual land fragments created by roads and bridge geometry.

### 2.3 Exterior Tower Photo

The exterior photo shows the twisting and leaning visual effect of the tower. The facade is composed of repeated residential modules and balconies. The building appears narrow at the base and broader at the upper levels.

This confirms the project’s main geometric transformation: a small triangular base evolves into a larger upper tower form.

## 5. Spatial Relationship Descriptions

### 5.1 Granville Bridge → constrains → Tower Base

The bridge and its required setbacks restrict the tower’s lower footprint, producing a small triangular base.

### 5.2 Tower Base → transforms into → Upper Floor Plate

The tower begins with a constrained triangular base and gradually expands upward into a more efficient residential floor plate.

### 5.3 Upper Floor Plate → maximizes → Residential Area

The larger upper levels reclaim usable floor area once the tower clears the bridge setback and traffic influence zone.

### 5.4 Twisting Tower → responds to → Infrastructure Geometry

The tower’s twisting form is generated by the need to negotiate the bridge, setbacks, site shape, and view conditions.

### 5.5 Mixed-use Podium → activates → Residual Urban Space

The podium and low-rise buildings use leftover spaces around the bridge for retail, restaurants, leisure, and public functions.

### 5.6 Public Realm → occupies → Bridge-Under Space

Spaces under and near the bridge are reinterpreted as public urban rooms rather than leftover infrastructure zones.

### 5.7 Residential Unit → opens to → Balcony

Apartment interiors connect to balconies or outdoor living spaces, creating a transition between private living and the city.

### 5.8 Living Area → faces → View

Living and dining areas are placed along the facade to capture views and daylight.

### 5.9 Balcony Module → repeats across → Tower Facade

Balcony and facade modules repeat across the tower, creating a residential grid and visual texture.

### 5.10 Tower → marks → Urban Gateway

The tower’s height and form make it a visual marker when entering Downtown Vancouver from Granville Bridge.

### 5.11 Low-rise Urban Village → mediates → Tower and Street

The low-rise buildings at the base connect the high-rise tower to the pedestrian scale of the street and bridge-under spaces.

### 5.12 Constraint Diagram → generates → Building Form

The building form is directly generated from site constraints, setbacks, shadow limits, bridge geometry, and real-estate optimization.

## 6. Overall Spatial Logic

Vancouver House’s spatial logic is based on transforming a difficult infrastructure site into a high-value residential tower and public urban village.

The main geometric sequence can be summarized as:

**Residual Triangular Site → Constrained Tower Base → Vertical Clearance → Expanding Floor Plate → Twisting Residential Tower**

The urban sequence can be summarized as:

**Bridge Infrastructure → Leftover Parcels → Mixed-use Podium Village → Public Realm Activation → Urban Gateway Tower**

The residential sequence can be summarized as:

**Entry / Service Zone → Living / Dining Space → Balcony / Outdoor Living → City / Water View**

Through this strategy, Vancouver House combines:

1. **Infrastructure response**
2. **Residential area optimization**
3. **Urban village activation**
4. **Skyline identity**
5. **View-oriented high-rise living**

The tower’s dramatic form is therefore not arbitrary. It results from the transformation of constraints into spatial and economic opportunity.

---

## 7. Design Interpretation

The drawing analysis suggests that Vancouver House is a project about turning urban constraint into architectural form.

High-rise residential towers usually prefer clear, regular, rectangular sites. Vancouver House begins with the opposite condition: a fragmented site under and beside a major bridge. The project does not reject this condition. Instead, it uses the geometry of the infrastructure as the generator of the tower.

The most important design interpretation is:

**The tower grows out of constraint.**

The narrow base reflects setback and bridge conditions. The expanded upper floor plate reflects the recovery of developable area above the bridge zone. The mixed-use base transforms residual land into an active public district.
