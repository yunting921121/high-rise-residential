## 1. Purpose of Photos and Renders Preprocess

This preprocess translates photos, renders, and visual materials into textual descriptions for architectural precedent analysis.

The goal is to extract:

1. **Design vocabulary**
   Visible architectural elements, green terraces, geological facade, tower forms, public routes, and mixed-use urban features.

2. **Atmospheric description**
   The visual and spatial atmosphere created by the contrast between glass exterior, stone valley, greenery, stepped terraces, and public accessibility.

3. **Design intent reading**
   The possible design intention behind Valley’s mixed-use program, public green route, and geological landscape identity.

4. **Contextual relationship**
   How the building relates to Amsterdam Zuidas, office-district transformation, public life, and urban biodiversity.

## 3. Atmospheric Description

Valley creates the atmosphere of a carved green mountain placed inside a business district.

From the exterior photo, the building has a strong contrast between two characters. The outer edges are smooth and glassy, relating to the corporate architecture of Zuidas. The internal valley is rough, planted, stepped, and geological. This contrast makes the building feel like a glass block that has been broken open to reveal a natural stone landscape inside.

The atmosphere can be described through five qualities:

### 3.1 Geological Urbanism

The building appears like a rocky formation. The stone surfaces, irregular terraces, and planted edges create the image of a man-made mountain.

### 3.2 Public Green Interior

Unlike closed office towers, Valley contains a publicly accessible green route that brings people into the building and up to the elevated valley.

### 3.3 Live-Work-Visit Mix

The building combines apartments, offices, shops, restaurants, cultural spaces, public routes, and a sky bar. This creates a lively environment beyond office hours.

### 3.4 Biodiverse Facade

The terraces are planted with thousands of plants, trees, and shrubs. The building aims to become greener over time as the planting matures.

### 3.5 Contrast with Zuidas

Valley contrasts with the polished and corporate atmosphere of Zuidas by introducing irregularity, greenery, stone, public access, and domestic life.

## 5. Light and Environmental Experience

Valley’s environmental experience is shaped by its terraced geometry and planting.

The environmental logic includes:

1. **Terraces create outdoor space.**
   The stepped geometry creates many planted ledges and balconies.

2. **Planting creates microclimate.**
   Trees and plants provide shade, humidity, visual comfort, and biodiversity.

3. **Stone surfaces create thermal and visual weight.**
   The natural stone gives the inner valley a geological identity.

4. **Public route creates lived landscape.**
   The valley is not only seen from apartments; it is walked through by the public.

5. **Different heights create varied wind and sunlight conditions.**
   Planting selection must respond to microclimatic differences across height and orientation.

Valley therefore treats the high-rise facade as a living landscape system rather than a simple vertical surface.

## 6. Spatial Hierarchy

The photos and diagrams suggest a clear spatial hierarchy:

### 6.1 City / Business District Layer

The building is located in the corporate Zuidas district.

### 6.2 Mixed-use Base Layer

The ground and lower levels contain offices, retail, food, cultural programs, parking, and entrances.

### 6.3 Public Valley Layer

The fourth and fifth floors contain the publicly accessible green valley.

### 6.4 Office Layer

Office floors occupy the lower levels, connecting the building to Zuidas’s working environment.

### 6.5 Residential Tower Layer

Apartments occupy the upper levels of the three towers.

### 6.6 Sky Bar Layer

The top of the tallest tower contains a public destination with panoramic views.

The hierarchy can be summarized as:

**Zuidas Business District → Mixed-use Base → Public Green Valley → Offices → Residential Towers → Sky Bar**

## 7. Design Intent Reading

From the exterior image, plans, section, and program diagram, Valley can be interpreted as a project that redefines the business district through mixed-use landscape urbanism.

The design intent can be understood in six main ways:

### 7.1 Humanizing the office district

The building introduces housing, greenery, public routes, and social programs into the formal business environment of Zuidas.

### 7.2 Making the high-rise publicly accessible

The green valley and external stairs allow public movement into the building, unlike many closed corporate towers.

### 7.3 Creating a natural interior within a glass exterior

The outer glass shell relates to the business district, while the inner stone valley creates a more natural and inviting environment.

### 7.4 Mixing living, working, and visiting

Valley is designed for residents, office workers, shoppers, visitors, and sky bar users.

### 7.5 Turning terraces into ecological infrastructure

The planted terraces support biodiversity, wellbeing, microclimate, and visual identity.

### 7.6 Creating uniqueness within density

Each apartment has a unique floor plan, and the building form creates varied terraces, views, and outdoor spaces.

## 8. Contextual Relationship

Valley relates to its context in four main ways:

### 8.1 Relationship with Zuidas

The project responds to Zuidas’s corporate character while challenging it with greenery, public access, and mixed-use life.

### 8.2 Relationship with Beethovenstraat

The building sits on Beethovenstraat and contributes an active frontage and distinctive landmark to the street.

### 8.3 Relationship with Sportpark Goed Genoeg

The nearby sports fields provide an open green condition that contrasts with the dense office district and supports the building’s green identity.

### 8.4 Relationship with Amsterdam’s urban transformation

Valley supports the municipality’s desire to turn Zuidas into a more complete urban district for living, working, and visiting.

### 9.3 Possible Design Issues

* Office districts can become mono-functional and inactive outside working hours.
* High-rise mixed-use buildings can become closed and inaccessible to the public.
* Dense towers often lack greenery, biodiversity, and outdoor public space.
* Standard glass facades can feel cold, corporate, and disconnected from human scale.
* Green terraces in high-rises require complex microclimate and maintenance planning.

### 9.5 Possible Design Strategies

* Insert public routes into high-rise buildings.
* Mix housing, offices, retail, culture, catering, and public destinations.
* Use planted terraces to create a green vertical landscape.
* Contrast outer corporate facade with inner natural landscape.
* Design plant species according to microclimate.
* Use stone surfaces and irregular geometry to create geological identity.
* Place a sky bar at the top to make the tower publicly accessible.
* Use public staircases to connect street level to elevated landscapes.

## 10. Preprocess Output Summary

### Main Visual Character

Valley presents a mixed-use high-rise as a geological green landscape. Its image is defined by three towers, stone terraces, planted ledges, public routes, cantilevered apartments, and a contrast between glass exterior and green interior.

### Main Atmospheric Quality

The project creates an atmosphere of an urban mountain or canyon inside a business district. It feels public, green, social, irregular, and more human than the surrounding office environment.

### Main Design Reading

Valley uses mixed-use programming, public accessibility, and planted geological terraces to transform Zuidas from a business district into a more livable urban environment.
