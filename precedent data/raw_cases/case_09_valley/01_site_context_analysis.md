## 1. Urban Location & Positioning

Valley is located in Amsterdam’s Zuidas district, one of the city’s major business and financial areas. The project sits on Beethovenstraat, opposite the ABN AMRO headquarters and next to Sportpark Goed Genoeg, within the Ravel area of Zuidas.

The project occupies a strategic urban position because Zuidas has historically been characterized by office buildings, business towers, transport infrastructure, and a relatively formal commercial atmosphere. Valley responds to this condition by introducing a more mixed, green, and socially active urban environment.

The site can be understood through four major urban conditions:

1. **Business district context**
   Zuidas is an office-dominated district with banks, corporate headquarters, commercial buildings, and high-rise developments. Valley stands out by introducing housing, public routes, retail, cultural facilities, and extensive greenery.

2. **Mixed-use urban transition**
   The project supports the transformation of Zuidas from a mono-functional business district into a more diverse urban district for living, working, visiting, and leisure.

3. **Publicly accessible landscape condition**
   Unlike many closed office buildings in Zuidas, Valley includes a public route that winds upward through the green valley on the fourth and fifth floors.

4. **Infrastructure and district-edge condition**
   The project is located near large transport corridors, office blocks, sports fields, and new development areas, making it part of a broader urban transformation in Amsterdam Zuid.

Valley therefore functions not only as a high-rise residential and office complex, but also as an urban connector that brings public landscape and mixed-use activity into a business district.

## 3. Surrounding Context

| Direction | Surrounding Context                                                                                                    |
| --------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **East**  | Zuidas business district, Beethovenstraat, ABN AMRO headquarters, commercial offices, and formal corporate urban space |
| **West**  | Sports fields, green recreational areas, and open landscape beside the dense business district                         |
| **South** | Ravel and Zuidas development areas, mixed-use expansion, new housing, offices, and future urban growth                 |
| **North** | Gustav Mahlerlaan, public transport links, Zuidas office core, and high-density urban development                      |

The surrounding context shows a strong contrast between business district intensity and landscape openness. Valley’s design responds to this contrast by creating a building with two characters:

1. **Outer glass shell**
   The outer facades facing the business district are smoother, more reflective, and more aligned with the corporate context of Zuidas.

2. **Inner green valley**
   The internal carved-out spaces are rougher, more geological, planted, and accessible, creating a natural and public contrast to the office environment.

This makes Valley a strong example of high-rise design that responds to urban context by creating two different facade identities: one outward-facing and corporate, the other inward-facing and ecological.

## 4. Sunlight & Shadow Conditions

Amsterdam has a temperate northern European climate with moderate sunlight, frequent cloud cover, wind, and rainfall. In such a climate, daylight, shelter, wind protection, and usable outdoor space are important for residential and public comfort.

Valley’s design responds to sunlight and shadow through its stepped massing, terraces, and planted valley system.

Key sunlight and shadow implications include:

1. **Terraced setbacks**
   The building’s staggered geometry creates terraces with different degrees of sunlight, shade, wind, and exposure.

2. **Public valley levels**
   The green valley on the fourth and fifth floors receives light from above and between the three towers. This creates a semi-public elevated landscape.

3. **Multiple tower heights**
   The three towers of different heights create varied shadow patterns and allow sunlight to reach different parts of the building and surrounding public spaces.

4. **Plant selection by microclimate**
   The landscape design responds to changing conditions such as wind, sun, shade, temperature, and maintenance access.

5. **Outer and inner facade contrast**
   The outer glass facades reflect the business district, while the inner stone and planted valley uses depth, shadow, and greenery to create a more protected microclimate.

The project treats sunlight and shadow as part of its geological and landscape identity. Terraces, stone surfaces, planting, and stepped massing create different environmental zones across the building.

## 5. Views & Vistas

Valley creates several important view conditions:

### 5.1 City View

The upper residential towers offer long views over Amsterdam, Zuidas, and the surrounding urban landscape.

### 5.2 Valley View

Apartments, offices, and public spaces can look into the planted green valley between the towers. This creates an internal landscape view within a dense urban building.

### 5.3 Sports Field View

The western side has a more open relationship with Sportpark Goed Genoeg and nearby recreational fields.

### 5.4 Public Sky Bar View

The tallest tower includes a sky bar, allowing visitors to experience panoramic views over Amsterdam.

### 5.5 Street and Business District View

The outer glass facades relate visually to Beethovenstraat, ABN AMRO, and the corporate Zuidas skyline.

Valley’s view strategy is both outward and inward. It provides city views from the towers, but it also creates an internal landscape that becomes a major visual focus.

## 6. Design Implications

The site context suggests that Valley’s design strategy can be interpreted through six main ideas.

### 6.1 Bringing mixed-use life into a business district

Valley introduces residences, offices, retail, catering, cultural facilities, and a public route into an area previously dominated by offices.

### 6.2 Creating a public landscape inside a high-rise complex

The green valley on the fourth and fifth floors makes landscape accessible beyond ground level.

### 6.3 Contrasting corporate exterior with natural interior

The smooth outer glass shell responds to the business district, while the inner valley reveals stone, terraces, plants, and a more informal public atmosphere.

### 6.4 Using terraced massing to create outdoor spaces

The stepped geometry creates balconies, terraces, planted ledges, and outdoor spaces throughout the towers.

### 6.5 Making biodiversity part of high-rise living

The building integrates extensive planting, trees, shrubs, planters, bird and bat boxes, bee and insect hotels, and irrigation / facade maintenance systems.

### 6.6 Reframing Zuidas as a live-work-visit district

Valley supports Amsterdam’s aim to transform Zuidas from a business district into a more mixed, livable, and inclusive urban quarter.

## 7. Site Context Summary

Valley is located on Beethovenstraat in Amsterdam Zuidas, opposite the ABN AMRO headquarters and next to Sportpark Goed Genoeg.

The main site conditions are:

1. **Office-dominated business district**
   The surrounding urban fabric is strongly associated with corporate office development.

2. **Need for mixed-use transformation**
   Zuidas is evolving into a more complete urban district for living, working, visiting, and leisure.

3. **Adjacent recreational landscape**
   The nearby sports fields provide an open green contrast to the hard office district.

4. **Need for public accessibility**
   The building responds by creating a public pedestrian route through the elevated green valley.

5. **Need for greenery and human scale**
   The project counters the inhospitable business environment through terraces, planting, stone surfaces, and public spaces.

Valley responds by turning a mixed-use high-rise complex into a geological and ecological landscape. Its towers combine housing, offices, retail, culture, and public route systems, while the planted valley creates a new form of elevated public urban nature.

Therefore, Valley can be understood as a high-rise residential precedent that uses mixed-use programming, public accessibility, planted terraces, and geological massing to transform an office district into a more livable urban environment.
