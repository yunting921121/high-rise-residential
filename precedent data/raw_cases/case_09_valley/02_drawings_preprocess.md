## 1. Purpose of Drawing Preprocess

This drawing preprocess translates architectural drawings and diagrams into textual information for Precedent DNA analysis.

The purpose is to extract:

1. **Architectural vocabulary**
   Important spatial elements, program types, public routes, terrace systems, tower forms, valley spaces, and environmental devices.

2. **Spatial relationship descriptions**
   How housing, office, retail, culture, parking, public route, central valley, terraces, stairs, towers, and planting relate to each other.

3. **Overall spatial logic**
   The design logic behind Valley as a mixed-use high-rise that creates a public green landscape inside a dense business district.

---

## 2. Drawing Inventory

### 2.1 5th Floor Plan

The 5th floor plan shows an elevated level where office floors and public landscape interweave. The plan contains large office plates on the sides and a central landscaped valley with irregular paths, vegetation, stairs, bridges, and outdoor spaces.

This drawing is important because it shows the building’s public character. The green valley is not only an aesthetic void. It is a spatial route and landscape that connects different parts of the building.

### 2.2 Redistributed Program Diagram

The program diagram shows the building as a three-tower composition with redistributed functions.

The diagram indicates several program layers:

* Housing
* Office
* Valley
* Food / Beverage / Retail
* Culture
* Parking
* Bike parking
* Technical spaces

The diagram shows that Valley is not a single-use tower. It is a vertical mixed-use complex where offices occupy the lower levels, apartments occupy the upper levels, and public or semi-public programs are inserted into the base and valley zones.

### 2.3 Section Drawing

The section shows the relationship between the three towers, underground parking, office floors, residential floors, valley levels, terraces, and public landscape. The section reveals a deep vertical composition in which the green valley is carved between the tower volumes.

The section is important because it shows Valley as a building organized by section, not just by plan. Public paths, terraces, planting, and residential towers are stacked and connected vertically.

### 2.4 Exterior Photo

The exterior photo shows the contrast between the glassy outer facade and the rough, stone-like planted terraces of the inner valley. The towers appear as a cluster of geological forms rising from a mixed-use base.

The exterior confirms the drawing logic: Valley is a building with two identities, a smooth exterior shell and a craggy green interior landscape.

## 5. Spatial Relationship Descriptions

### 5.1 Three Towers → form → Mixed-use Cluster

The three towers of different heights form a mixed-use urban cluster rather than a single isolated tower.

### 5.2 Offices → occupy → Lower Floors

Office programs are located in the lower levels, connecting the building to Zuidas’s business district context.

### 5.3 Apartments → occupy → Upper Towers

Residential units are placed above the office levels, allowing them to access more light, air, and views.

### 5.4 Retail and Culture → activate → Ground Level

Retail, food, beverage, cultural, and public functions animate the base of the building and connect it to the street.

### 5.5 Green Valley → winds between → Towers

The green valley is carved between the tower volumes and becomes a publicly accessible landscape route.

### 5.6 Public Staircases → connect → Street and Valley

External stone staircases allow the public to move from street level to the elevated green valley.

### 5.7 Valley → organizes → Public Route

The valley is not only a garden but also a circulation route that brings visitors into and through the building.

### 5.8 Planted Terraces → extend → Apartments and Offices

Terraces with planting extend residential and office spaces outward and create exterior green environments.

### 5.9 Natural Stone Facade → creates → Geological Identity

Stone surfaces and irregular geometry create the impression of a carved geological landscape.

### 5.10 Outer Glass Shell → responds to → Business District

The smoother outer facade fits the formal glass character of the surrounding office district.

### 5.11 Inner Green Facade → contrasts with → Outer Shell

The inner valley facade is rough, planted, and inviting, creating a contrast with the corporate exterior.

### 5.12 Planting Matrix → adapts to → Microclimate

The landscape system uses different plant species according to wind, sun, shade, temperature, and maintenance conditions.

### 5.13 Sky Bar → offers → Panoramic View

The sky bar at the top of the tallest tower provides visitors with wide views over Amsterdam.

### 5.14 Grotto → forms → Covered Street

The Grotto atrium creates an indoor public space connected to skylights and the valley above.

## 6. Overall Spatial Logic

Valley’s spatial logic is based on carving a public green landscape into a mixed-use high-rise block.

The program sequence can be summarized as:

**Underground Parking → Retail / Culture / Office Base → Public Valley → Residential Towers → Sky Bar**

The landscape sequence can be summarized as:

**Street → Public Staircase → Green Valley → Planted Terraces → Tower Gardens**

The facade sequence can be summarized as:

**Outer Glass Shell → Craggy Stone Interior → Planted Terraces → Green Valley**

Through this strategy, Valley combines:

1. **Business district transformation**
2. **Mixed-use programming**
3. **Public accessibility**
4. **High-rise residential living**
5. **Green landscape integration**
6. **Geological architectural identity**

The project turns a dense mixed-use high-rise into a public landscape structure.

---

## 7. Design Interpretation

The drawing analysis suggests that Valley is a project about transforming a corporate business district into a more human, green, and mixed urban environment.

The most important design interpretation is:

**The void becomes the public landscape.**

Instead of treating the space between towers as leftover air, Valley carves it into a publicly accessible green valley. This void organizes circulation, public life, views, planting, and identity.

The building also uses contrast as a design strategy. The exterior shell is smooth and corporate, while the interior valley is rough, planted, and social. This contrast allows Valley to belong to Zuidas while also challenging its existing office-district character.
