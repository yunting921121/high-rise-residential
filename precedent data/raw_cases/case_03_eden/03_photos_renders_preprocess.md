## 1. Purpose of Photos and Renders Preprocess

This preprocess translates photos and rendered images into textual descriptions for architectural precedent analysis.

The goal is to extract:

1. **Design vocabulary**
   Visible architectural elements, facade components, balcony forms, greenery, materiality, and environmental design features.

2. **Atmospheric description**
   The visual and spatial atmosphere created by the tower’s earthy material, tropical planting, verticality, and private garden balconies.

3. **Design intent reading**
   The possible design intention behind the visible architectural expression.

4. **Contextual relationship**
   How the building relates to Singapore’s high-rise residential environment, tropical climate, and garden-city identity.

## 3. Atmospheric Description

EDEN creates the atmosphere of a private tropical house lifted into a high-rise tower.

From the exterior photo, the building appears as a tall, earth-toned vertical volume surrounded by other white and glass residential towers. Unlike many high-rise residential buildings that use smooth glass curtain walls, EDEN uses warm concrete surfaces, deep vertical slots, and planted balconies.

The overall atmosphere can be described through five qualities:

### 3.1 Tropical Domesticity

The building does not present itself as a cold urban tower. The planted balconies and earthy facade create a warmer, more domestic atmosphere. The tower suggests that each apartment has its own garden.

### 3.2 Vertical Garden Living

Greenery is inserted into the vertical facade, especially at balcony zones. This creates the impression that garden spaces are stacked throughout the tower.

### 3.3 Privacy and Retreat

The planted balconies create visual separation between the apartment interiors and the surrounding city. The building feels private and protected despite being located in a dense residential district.

### 3.4 Earthy Material Character

The warm brown concrete facade gives the building a grounded and tactile quality. This differs from the reflective glass surfaces common in many luxury towers.

### 3.5 Biophilic Identity

The project’s identity is defined by the combination of architecture and planting. The greenery is not decorative only; it is central to the spatial and atmospheric experience of the residences.

## 5. Light and Environmental Experience

Singapore’s tropical climate requires protection from intense sun, heat, glare, and humidity. EDEN responds through deep balconies, facade depth, and vegetation.

The garden balconies work as environmental filters:

1. **They shade interior spaces.**
   The balcony depth and planting reduce direct solar exposure.

2. **They filter views.**
   Vegetation creates a semi-transparent screen between interior rooms and the surrounding city.

3. **They create outdoor comfort.**
   Planted balconies provide shaded outdoor areas that can be used as extensions of the apartment.

4. **They support natural ventilation.**
   The full-floor layout allows multiple orientations, which can improve the potential for cross-ventilation.

5. **They create a microclimate buffer.**
   Planting helps soften heat, glare, and the hardness of the surrounding urban environment.

The building therefore uses the balcony not only as a luxury feature, but also as an environmental and spatial device.

## 6. Spatial Hierarchy

The photos and drawings suggest a clear spatial hierarchy:

### 6.1 City Layer

The project is located in Singapore’s dense high-rise residential environment, close to Orchard Road and the Ardmore / Draycott residential enclave.

### 6.2 Tower Layer

The slender tower creates a vertical stack of private residences.

### 6.3 Full-Floor Apartment Layer

Each apartment occupies an entire floor and functions like a private house in the sky.

### 6.4 Central Domestic Layer

The central living and dining space organizes the apartment interior.

### 6.5 Garden Balcony Layer

Tropical plants filter sunlight, views, heat, and privacy between the apartment and the city.

The overall hierarchy can be summarized as:

**City → Tower → Full-Floor Apartment → Central Living Space → Garden Balcony → Vegetation Buffer**

## 7. Design Intent Reading

From the exterior photo, plan, and section, EDEN can be interpreted as a project that redefines luxury high-rise living through the idea of the garden home.

The design intent can be understood in five main ways:

### 7.1 Bringing the private garden into the high-rise

The project attempts to give each apartment the feeling of a house with a garden, even though the residences are stacked in a tower.

### 7.2 Creating privacy in a dense residential district

The surrounding area contains many high-rise residential buildings. Garden balconies and deep facade walls help protect residents from direct visual exposure.

### 7.3 Responding to tropical climate

The building uses shaded balconies, planting, and facade depth to reduce heat gain and create cooler outdoor thresholds.

### 7.4 Moving away from the glass tower typology

Instead of using a sealed glass facade, EDEN uses textured concrete, deep openings, and greenery to create a more tactile and climate-responsive tower.

### 7.5 Extending Singapore’s garden-city idea vertically

The project lifts greenery from the ground plane into each apartment level, creating a vertical version of garden living.

---

## 8. Contextual Relationship

EDEN relates to its Singapore context in three main ways:

### 8.1 Relationship with the tropical city

The project responds to Singapore’s climate through shading, greenery, facade depth, and potential natural ventilation.

### 8.2 Relationship with the residential enclave

The project is located in a high-end residential district. Its full-floor apartments, private lift access, and garden balconies create a sense of exclusivity and privacy.

### 8.3 Relationship with the garden-city identity

Singapore is strongly associated with greenery and the idea of a city in a garden. EDEN translates this identity into a high-rise residential form by placing gardens on every residential level.
