## 1. Purpose of Drawing Preprocess

This drawing preprocess translates architectural drawings and diagrams into textual information for Precedent DNA analysis.

The purpose is to extract:

1. **Architectural vocabulary**
   Important spatial elements, apartment components, structural systems, material systems, balconies, terraces, and sustainability devices.

2. **Spatial relationship descriptions**
   How the triangular floor plan, central core, apartments, balconies, timber structure, concrete base, steel framing, and residential spaces relate to each other.

3. **Overall spatial logic**
   The design logic behind HAUT as a hybrid timber high-rise tower that combines sustainable construction, flexible apartments, and riverfront residential living.

## 2. Drawing Inventory

### 2.1 First Floor / Residential Floor Plan

The residential plan shows a triangular or wedge-like floor plate organized around a central elevator and stair core. Apartment units are arranged around the perimeter, giving rooms access to daylight, balconies, and views.

The plan indicates several residential components:

1. Commercial space
2. Living room
3. Bedroom
4. Kitchen / dining room
5. Bathroom
6. Balcony / roof terrace
7. Roof garden / communal green
8. Elevator hall
9. Escaped stairwell

This plan shows that HAUT is not a simple rectangular tower. The building uses an irregular triangular geometry, producing different apartment types and balcony conditions.

### 2.2 Structural Composition Diagram

The structural diagram explains the main support structure of HAUT through three material systems:

1. **Concrete base**
   The lower base, foundation, basement, and core rely on concrete for stability, fire safety, stiffness, and ground contact.

2. **CLT / glulam timber structure**
   The main residential structure uses cross-laminated timber panels, glulam elements, and timber-based floor / wall systems.

3. **Steel framing**
   Steel supports specific cantilevered, balcony, and edge conditions where additional strength or span is required.

This diagram is important because it shows HAUT as a hybrid building rather than an all-timber tower. The project combines materials according to performance needs.

### 2.3 Exterior Photo

The exterior photo shows a layered facade with balconies, horizontal floor edges, glass openings, and staggered residential volumes. The tower has a modern appearance but is structurally connected to timber innovation.

The photo confirms the spatial logic of the plan: residential units are arranged along the perimeter, balconies and terraces shape the facade, and the tower’s irregular geometry creates varied apartment conditions.

## 5. Spatial Relationship Descriptions

### 5.1 Central Core → serves → Apartment Units

The elevator hall, stair core, and escape stairwell organize vertical circulation and provide access to apartments.

### 5.2 Apartment Units → occupy → Perimeter

Apartment units are arranged around the exterior perimeter to maximize daylight, views, and access to balconies.

### 5.3 Living Room → opens to → Balcony

Living spaces connect to balconies or terraces, creating an indoor-outdoor relationship.

### 5.4 Balcony → extends → Residential Unit

Balconies and terraces extend the apartment outward and create private outdoor spaces.

### 5.5 Triangular Floor Plate → creates → Apartment Variation

The irregular triangular floor plate generates different apartment sizes, shapes, orientations, and balcony conditions.

### 5.6 Concrete Core → stabilizes → Timber Tower

The concrete core provides stiffness, stability, and fire-safe vertical circulation for the timber high-rise.

### 5.7 Concrete Base → supports → Timber Structure

The concrete base and foundation support the timber residential structure above and respond to Amsterdam’s wet soil and high groundwater conditions.

### 5.8 CLT / Glulam Structure → forms → Residential Stack

Cross-laminated timber panels and glulam elements form the main timber structural system of the residential floors.

### 5.9 Steel Framing → reinforces → Cantilevered Edges

Steel framing supports edge conditions, cantilevers, and facade or balcony areas where additional strength is required.

### 5.10 Roof Garden → caps → Residential Tower

The rooftop garden completes the sustainable living concept and provides shared green space at the top.

### 5.11 Commercial Plinth → activates → Ground Level

The commercial base connects the residential tower to street-level urban activity.

### 5.12 Solar Panels → generate → Renewable Energy

Solar panels on roof and facade contribute to the building’s environmental performance.

## 6. Overall Spatial Logic

HAUT’s spatial logic is based on combining a compact high-rise residential plan with a hybrid timber structural system.

The residential sequence can be summarized as:

**Central Core → Apartment Unit → Living / Bedroom Space → Balcony / Terrace → River or City View**

The structural sequence can be summarized as:

**Concrete Foundation / Base / Core → CLT and Glulam Residential Structure → Steel Edge Reinforcement → Non-load-bearing Timber Frame Facade**

The sustainability sequence can be summarized as:

**Timber Carbon Storage → Solar Energy → Ground Cooling → Low-temperature Floor Heating / Cooling → Rainwater Storage → BREEAM Outstanding Performance**

Through this strategy, HAUT combines:

1. **High-rise residential density**
2. **Hybrid timber innovation**
3. **Flexible apartment planning**
4. **Riverfront views**
5. **Balconies and terraces**
6. **Low-carbon construction**
7. **Sustainable technical systems**

The project demonstrates how high-rise residential architecture can use timber as a primary material while still relying on concrete and steel where performance demands require it.

---

## 7. Design Interpretation

The drawing analysis suggests that HAUT is a project about making sustainable high-rise housing technically and spatially possible.

The most important design interpretation is:

**The tower is a hybrid system rather than a pure timber object.**

This hybridity is crucial. Concrete is used where the tower needs stability, grounding, fire-safe circulation, and basement performance. Timber is used where the building can reduce carbon impact, create a lighter structure, and demonstrate renewable material construction. Steel is used for specific edge and cantilever conditions.

The apartment planning also supports this logic. Instead of creating identical repetitive units, the triangular floor plate and flexible layout options produce varied residences with balconies, loggias, terraces, and large windows.

HAUT is therefore a useful precedent for high-rise housing where structural sustainability, material innovation, and residential quality are equally important.
