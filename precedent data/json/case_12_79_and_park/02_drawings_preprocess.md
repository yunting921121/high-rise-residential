## 1. Purpose of Drawing Preprocess

This drawing preprocess translates architectural drawings into textual information for Precedent DNA analysis.

The purpose is to extract:

1. **Architectural vocabulary**
   Important spatial elements, modular units, courtyard spaces, terrace systems, roof gardens, facade components, and circulation devices.

2. **Spatial relationship descriptions**
   How modules, apartments, terraces, courtyard, roof landscape, facade, street, and park relate to each other.

3. **Overall spatial logic**
   The design logic behind 79 & Park as a pixelated residential block that becomes an inhabitable hillside at the edge of a park.

---

## 2. Drawing Inventory

### 2.1 Section Drawing

The section drawing shows the building as a stepped mass surrounding a central courtyard. One side of the building rises higher, while the opposite side steps down toward a lower edge.

The section reveals three important relationships:

1. The building is organized as a courtyard block.
2. The roof becomes an inhabitable stepped landscape.
3. The internal courtyard receives light and visual openness because of the sloped massing.

The section also shows trees and planting on upper terraces, indicating that the roof is not only a technical surface but also an outdoor residential landscape.

### 2.2 Street Facade Drawings

The east and west facade drawings show a stepped facade with repetitive window modules, balconies, and bay-like protrusions.

The facade is not flat. It is composed of repeated units that shift in height and depth. This creates a pixelated and terraced image.

The facade drawings also reveal that the building has two different urban readings:

* A more urban street facade facing the city.
* A softer, stepped landscape-like profile toward the park and courtyard.

### 2.3 Ground Floor Plan

The ground floor plan shows a perimeter block surrounding a central green courtyard. Apartments and common areas are arranged along the edges, while the courtyard functions as an internal communal landscape.

The plan also shows diagonal and repeated modular geometry. The building is not a simple rectangular block. Instead, the units are composed through a grid-like system that creates a jagged edge and varied apartment layouts.

The courtyard contains planting, paths, open areas, and outdoor shared spaces. This makes the inner void a social and environmental center of the project.

### 2.4 Exterior Photo

The exterior photo shows the project as a terraced hillside rising from the park edge. Wood cladding, roof gardens, and stepped modules reinforce the image of a building that mediates between nature and urban density.

The photo confirms the drawing logic: the building uses massing, material, and vegetation to become a residential landscape.

## 5. Spatial Relationship Descriptions

### 5.1 Perimeter Block → surrounds → Central Courtyard

The building forms a courtyard block that wraps around a shared green courtyard.

### 5.2 Central Courtyard → organizes → Residential Community

The courtyard becomes the communal heart of the project and provides shared landscape space for residents.

### 5.3 Modular Units → generate → Pixelated Massing

Repeated square modules create a pixelated form with varied apartment layouts, terrace depths, and facade conditions.

### 5.4 Raised Corner → creates → Urban Landmark

The higher corner gives the project a stronger city-facing presence and creates broader views.

### 5.5 Lowered Corner → brings → Daylight to Courtyard

The lower corner allows more daylight to enter the central courtyard and reduces enclosure.

### 5.6 Roof Terraces → extend → Apartment Units

Roof terraces extend apartment living outdoors and create private or shared open spaces.

### 5.7 Roof Landscape → mediates → Park and Housing

The stepped roof landscape makes the building feel like an extension of the surrounding park.

### 5.8 Wood Facade → softens → Residential Block

The wood cladding gives the large block a warmer, more natural character.

### 5.9 Bay Windows → frame → Views

Bay windows and facade projections create view conditions toward the park, city, courtyard, and harbor.

### 5.10 Public Passages → connect → Street and Courtyard

Open passages allow the courtyard to connect more directly with the surrounding urban context.

### 5.11 Apartments → occupy → Perimeter

Apartment units occupy the building perimeter so that rooms can access daylight, views, and outdoor space.

### 5.12 Terraced Massing → balances → Density and Landscape

The stepped building form allows the project to maintain urban density while creating a landscape-like profile.

## 6. Overall Spatial Logic

79 & Park’s spatial logic is based on transforming a courtyard block into a hillside.

The massing sequence can be summarized as:

**Regular Grid → Perimeter Block → Porous Courtyard → Raised Corner / Lowered Corner → Terraced Hillside**

The residential sequence can be summarized as:

**Apartment Unit → Window / Bay → Balcony or Terrace → Courtyard / Park View**

The landscape sequence can be summarized as:

**Gärdet Park → Building Edge → Central Courtyard → Roof Terraces → Planted Roof Landscape**

Through this strategy, 79 & Park combines:

1. **Urban residential density**
2. **Park-edge landscape continuity**
3. **Central courtyard community**
4. **Private and shared outdoor terraces**
5. **Pixelated modular construction**
6. **Daylight-sensitive massing**
7. **Domestic-scale facade expression**

The project demonstrates how a residential block can become both a building and a landscape.

---

## 7. Design Interpretation

The drawing analysis suggests that 79 & Park is a project about translating landscape into residential massing.

The most important design interpretation is:

**The block becomes a hill.**

Instead of creating a flat courtyard block, the building uses modular units to form a stepped terrain. Apartments become part of a residential landscape where roofs, terraces, windows, courtyards, and park views are all connected.

This makes 79 & Park a useful precedent for high-rise and mid-rise housing where the goal is to combine density, domestic outdoor space, courtyard community, and park-edge sensitivity.
